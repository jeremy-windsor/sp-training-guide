# 9.3 — OTN (Optical Transport Network) — Answer Key

---

## Question 1
> **Scenario**: A wholesale carrier sells an ODU2 service across three providers. The end customer reports intermittent errors. PM counters show occasional BBE but no SES. How would you use TCM to isolate which provider's segment is causing the errors?

### Answer

This is exactly the scenario OTN's Tandem Connection Monitoring was built for. Three providers = three TCM levels. Here's how you isolate it.

**Setup (should already be provisioned if the wholesale contract is competent):**

```
Customer A ←→ [Provider 1] ←→ [Provider 2] ←→ [Provider 3] ←→ Customer B
                  TCM1              TCM2              TCM3
              PM (end-to-end path monitoring)
```

Each provider terminates their TCM level at their ingress and egress OTN nodes. End-to-end PM runs Customer A → Customer B.

**Isolation procedure:**

1. **Pull PM counters at both endpoints.** You already know PM shows BBE (Background Block Errors) but no SES (Severely Errored Seconds). BBE without SES means the error rate is above zero but below the SES threshold (typically BER > 1e-6 for more than 1 second). This is a low-grade degradation, not a hard fault.

2. **Pull TCM1, TCM2, and TCM3 counters from each provider's NMS.** Each TCM level has its own independent BIP-8 error counter, BEI (Backward Error Indication), and trail trace. Compare BBE counts across all three TCM levels for the same 15-minute PM intervals.

3. **The TCM with elevated BBE is the guilty segment.** If TCM2 shows BBE but TCM1 and TCM3 show zero, Provider 2's segment is degrading. It's that simple — and that's why TCM exists.

**Subtleties:**

- **BBE can cascade.** If Provider 1 introduces bit errors, those errors propagate through Provider 2 and Provider 3's segments. However, each TCM only counts errors *within its span*. TCM1 counts errors between its sink and source points. If TCM1 shows BBE but TCM2 and TCM3 are clean, the errors originated in Provider 1's segment — the downstream providers faithfully transported the already-errored signal without adding new errors.

- **BEI matters.** Each TCM has a backward error indication channel. Provider 1's egress node sends BEI back to Provider 1's ingress node, telling it "I received X errored blocks from you." If Provider 1's NMS shows their TCM1 BEI count rising, they know their own segment is the problem — they don't need you to tell them.

- **Trail Trace Identifier (TTI) verification.** Before blaming any provider, verify that TTI strings match expected values on all TCM levels. A TTI mismatch means the OTN path has been misconnected — you might be monitoring the wrong circuit entirely.

- **Timing matters.** Pull PM data at 15-minute and 24-hour granularity. Intermittent errors may correlate with time of day (thermal effects on fiber), maintenance windows, or traffic patterns.

**What if all three TCMs show BBE?** Then either (a) errors are originating at the customer demarcation (CPE/NTE issue), or (b) you have a PM/TCM configuration error and the monitoring boundaries aren't where you think they are. Verify the TCM source/sink node assignments.

## Question 2
> **Design Decision**: Your network carries 40 × 10GbE clients on a long-haul 400G wavelength. Would you use 4 × ODU4 with ODU2 multiplexing, or a single OTUC4 with ODUflex? What are the trade-offs in management complexity, bandwidth efficiency, and failure granularity?

### Answer

Let's break this down. 40 × 10GbE = 400G of client traffic on a single 400G wavelength. Two approaches:

**Option A: 4 × ODU4 with ODU2 multiplexing**

Each ODU4 carries ~100G. You'd map 10 × 10GbE clients into 10 × ODU2, then multiplex those 10 ODU2s into one ODU4. Repeat four times: 4 × ODU4 = 40 × 10GbE.

```
10GbE → ODU2 ──┐
10GbE → ODU2 ──┤
  ...          ├──→ ODU4 (10 × ODU2) ──┐
10GbE → ODU2 ──┘                       │
                                        ├──→ 400G wavelength
10GbE → ODU2 ──┐                       │    (4 × ODU4)
  ...          ├──→ ODU4 (10 × ODU2) ──┤
10GbE → ODU2 ──┘                       │
  ... (×4)                              ┘
```

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Management complexity | High — 4 ODU4 containers, 40 ODU2 tributaries, full OTN multiplexing hierarchy. Each level has its own PM, TCM, protection, and overhead. |
| Bandwidth efficiency | Moderate — ODU2 payload is 10.037 Gbps for a 10.3125 Gbps 10GbE LAN PHY signal. You'd actually need ODU2e for native 10GbE mapping, or use GFP adaptation into standard ODU2 (slight bandwidth waste from rate justification). |
| Failure granularity | Excellent — you can protect, monitor, and reroute at the individual ODU2 level. If one client fails, 39 others are unaffected. You can provision/deprovision individual ODU2s without touching the others. |
| Grooming flexibility | High — at intermediate OTN switching nodes, you can add/drop individual ODU2s to serve local clients without disturbing pass-through traffic. |

**Option B: Single OTUC4 with ODUflex**

Map all 40 × 10GbE clients into ODUflex containers within a single OTUC4 frame. ODUflex(GFP) provides variable-rate containers sized to each client's needs.

```
10GbE → ODUflex ──┐
10GbE → ODUflex ──┤
  ...             ├──→ OTUC4 (single 400G container)
10GbE → ODUflex ──┤
10GbE → ODUflex ──┘
```

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Management complexity | Lower — one OTUC4 container, 40 ODUflex tributaries. Flatter hierarchy, fewer layers of overhead. |
| Bandwidth efficiency | Better — ODUflex sizes exactly to the client rate. No wasted bandwidth from fixed ODU2 container sizes. |
| Failure granularity | Good but different — ODUflex provides per-client isolation, but intermediate grooming is more complex. A single OTUC4 failure takes all 40 clients down. |
| Grooming flexibility | Lower — OTUC4 is a monolithic container at the line side. Intermediate nodes need OTUC4 termination capability to groom individual ODUflex tributaries. |

**My recommendation:** For a long-haul point-to-point link with no intermediate grooming needs, **OTUC4 with ODUflex**. Simpler management, better bandwidth efficiency, and you don't need the intermediate add/drop flexibility that classical ODU multiplexing provides.

For a multi-segment path where intermediate OTN switching nodes need to add/drop individual 10GbE clients, **4 × ODU4 with ODU2 multiplexing**. The hierarchical structure enables efficient grooming without terminating the entire 400G container.

**The real answer in 2026:** Increasingly, neither. The industry trend is to skip OTN entirely for this use case — map 40 × 10GbE into an IP/MPLS router, aggregate into 400GbE, and light a 400G coherent pluggable directly. OTN grooming only wins when you have a genuine multi-operator, multi-domain requirement for per-client PM/TCM or when you're a wholesale carrier selling sub-wavelength services.

## Question 3
> **Troubleshooting**: After a fiber splice repair, your OTU4 link comes back up but pre-FEC BER is at 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ (RS(255,239) threshold is 1.8 × 10⁻⁴). The path was previously running at 2 × 10⁻⁵. What do you investigate, and would you accept this margin for a production circuit?

### Answer

**Absolutely not.** You have 0.8 dB of margin between your current BER and the FEC cliff. That's one connector degradation, one temperature swing, or one new wavelength being added away from a hard outage. This is a ticking time bomb.

**The math:** Pre-existing BER was 2 × 10⁻⁵. After the splice repair, it's 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ — nearly an order of magnitude worse. The splice repair introduced approximately 1.5–2 dB of additional penalty (rough conversion: each ~0.5 dB of OSNR degradation roughly doubles BER in this regime, depending on modulation and operating point).

**Investigation plan:**

1. **OTDR the repaired span.** Compare the splice loss at the repair point to the pre-repair OTDR trace (you do have one, right?). A good fusion splice should be <0.1 dB. A field splice in bad conditions can be 0.3–1.0 dB. If the repair splice is >0.3 dB, the tech needs to redo it.

2. **Check for reflections.** A mechanical splice or poor fusion splice can introduce reflections (return loss). Coherent receivers are somewhat tolerant, but discrete reflections can cause MPI (multi-path interference) that increases BER without significantly affecting OSNR measurements.

3. **Verify fiber continuity.** Did the repair crew accidentally swap fibers? TX connected to TX instead of TX-to-RX? This sounds basic, but it happens more often than any optical engineer will admit publicly.

4. **Check for macro-bends.** Excess fiber coiled in the splice enclosure may have tight bend radii. Bend loss is wavelength-dependent and can selectively degrade certain channels more than others.

5. **Compare OSNR before and after.** If OSNR dropped by ~2 dB at the receiver, the splice loss plus any associated connector/patch panel losses from the repair explains the BER degradation directly.

6. **Check the splice enclosure environment.** Water ingress, thermal exposure, or physical strain on the fiber can cause ongoing degradation.

**Would I accept this margin?** No. Standard practice requires a minimum system margin of 3 dB above the FEC threshold for a production circuit. At 1.5 × 10⁻⁴ vs a threshold of 1.8 × 10⁻⁴, you have less than 1 dB of margin. Any of the following common events would push you over the FEC cliff and cause a hard outage:

- Seasonal temperature swing (0.5–1 dB)
- Adjacent channel addition requiring power rebalancing (0.3–0.5 dB)
- Amplifier aging over 2–3 years (0.5–1 dB)
- Another connector cleaning or fiber event (0.2–0.5 dB)

**Required action:** The splice must be redone to achieve <0.15 dB loss, or the span needs additional Raman assist, or the channel needs to be moved to a lower modulation format with better FEC margin. Do not put this circuit back into production at current BER.

## Question 4
> **Architecture**: A greenfield SP is building a national backbone. They will be the sole operator (no wholesale). Should they deploy OTN, or go pure IPoDWDM with coherent pluggables? Argue both sides.

### Answer

**The case FOR OTN:**

1. **Sub-wavelength grooming.** Even without wholesale, you'll have traffic flows that don't fill a full wavelength. A 10GbE private line between two cities doesn't need a dedicated 400G λ. OTN lets you multiplex 40 of these into one wavelength efficiently. Without OTN, you're either wasting 390G per wavelength or forcing everything through the router layer (burning expensive router port capacity on simple transport).

2. **Optical layer protection.** OTN provides SNCP (1+1) and shared ring protection at the ODU level with <50ms switchover. This is independent of IP-layer protection and provides defense-in-depth. For a national backbone where a single fiber cut can affect millions of customers, having a protection layer below IP is real insurance.

3. **Performance monitoring depth.** OTN PM (BIP-8, BBE, SES, ES, UAS per ODU and OTU) gives you continuous, standards-based health monitoring of every path segment. With IPoDWDM, your monitoring is limited to what the coherent pluggable and the router interface report — which is less granular.

4. **Future wholesale optionality.** You say "sole operator" today. In 3 years, if you want to sell wavelengths or sub-wavelength services to enterprise customers, OTN's TCM and PM framework is the commercial-grade toolkit that enables SLA reporting and fault isolation per customer.

5. **Technology evolution independence.** OTN separates the client rate from the line rate. When you upgrade from 400G to 800G wavelengths, the OTN switch handles the remapping — your client interfaces don't change.

**The case AGAINST OTN (pure IPoDWDM):**

1. **Complexity and cost.** OTN switching shelves (Infinera XTM, Ciena 6500, Nokia 1830) are expensive, power-hungry, and require specialized staff. A greenfield SP could spend that capex on more router capacity and coherent pluggables.

2. **Grooming at Layer 3 is fine.** Modern routers handle 10GbE/100GbE aggregation natively. You don't need OTN to pack 40 × 10GbE onto a 400G wavelength — the router does it in the forwarding ASIC. The "grooming" argument for OTN only holds if you're transporting non-IP services (Fibre Channel, SONET, video), which a greenfield IP backbone probably isn't.

3. **Operational simplicity.** One management domain (IP/MPLS + coherent optics managed through the router) vs two (IP NMS + optical NMS). One vendor relationship per site instead of two. One sparing inventory. One troubleshooting workflow. This is enormous for a startup SP with a small team.

4. **IPoDWDM reach is sufficient.** With OpenZR+ and CFP2-DCO, pluggable coherent covers metro (400ZR, <120 km) and regional (OpenZR+, <800 km). For long-haul, deploy a disaggregated open line system (amplifiers + ROADMs only) and let the router's pluggable coherent do the transponder job. No standalone transponders, no OTN.

5. **The industry is going this way.** Every major router vendor (Cisco, Juniper/Ericsson, Nokia) is pushing routed optical networking. The 800ZR/ZR+ pluggables arriving in 2026–2027 will extend IPoDWDM reach to most national backbone distances. Deploying OTN in a greenfield build is swimming against the tide.

**My verdict:** For a greenfield national backbone with no wholesale requirement, **go IPoDWDM**. Use an open DWDM line system (amplifiers + flex-grid ROADMs from Ciena or Nokia) with coherent pluggables in your routers. Reserve OTN for the day you actually need sub-wavelength wholesale services — and by then, the OTN switch market will be cheaper and more mature anyway.

The only exception: if you're carrying non-IP traffic (legacy TDM, Fibre Channel, OTT video transport) that doesn't naturally aggregate through an IP router. In that case, OTN grooming earns its keep. But in a greenfield 2026 build, that scenario is increasingly rare.
