# 9.4 — Packet-Optical Integration — Answer Key

---

## Question 1
> **Scenario**: Your SR-TE policy provides SRLG-diverse protection for a financial customer. After a fiber cut, the backup path also fails. What went wrong, and how do you prevent it?

### Answer

Your SRLG database is wrong. Full stop.

**What happened:** Shared Risk Link Groups (SRLGs) are supposed to represent physical shared fate — links that share fiber, conduit, duct, bridge, or right-of-way. Your SR-TE controller computed a backup path that *appeared* SRLG-diverse based on the SRLG database, but the actual physical infrastructure wasn't diverse. The fiber cut took both the primary and backup because they shared physical infrastructure that wasn't captured in the SRLG model.

**Common causes of SRLG database inaccuracy:**

1. **Initial provisioning error.** Someone assigned SRLG IDs based on logical topology maps, not physical fiber route surveys. "These two links go to different routers" ≠ "these two links are on different fiber."

2. **Post-deployment fiber rerouting.** A construction project forced the fiber provider to reroute one span. The new route now shares conduit with another span. Nobody updated the SRLG database.

3. **Bridge/river crossing convergence.** Two "diverse" routes that converge to cross the same bridge, tunnel, or river crossing. This is the classic gotcha — the Section 9.1 war story about the gas line fire.

4. **Third-party fiber provider abstraction.** You lease dark fiber or wavelengths from a carrier. They tell you the routes are diverse. They might not be. Unless you've physically verified the routes, you're trusting their documentation.

5. **Incomplete SRLG granularity.** SRLGs were defined at the span level (Router A → Router B) but not at the sub-span level (conduit segment X shares a trench with conduit segment Y for 2 km).

**How to prevent it:**

| Prevention Measure | Implementation |
|-------------------|----------------|
| **Physical fiber route verification** | GIS-based diversity analysis at the duct/conduit level. Not logical — physical. Walk the route if you have to. |
| **Automated SRLG discovery** | Some optical controllers (Ciena MCP, Nokia NSP) can correlate fiber route data with SRLG assignments. Feed them actual fiber route GPS coordinates. |
| **SRLG audit cycle** | Quarterly review of SRLG database against fiber provider route documentation. Any fiber maintenance event triggers SRLG re-verification. |
| **Multi-layer SRLG propagation** | Optical layer publishes SRLGs to the IP layer via BGP-LS or YANG models. If the optical network knows two wavelengths share a conduit, the IP layer should too. |
| **Diverse fiber provider** | For truly critical services, source primary and backup paths from different fiber providers. Different conduit, different right-of-way, different everything. Expensive but real. |
| **Minimum separation distance** | Define a minimum physical separation (e.g., 20 miles) between any two "diverse" paths at all points. Verify with GIS. |

**For the financial customer specifically:** They should be told the truth — SRLG diversity failed because the data was wrong. The remediation is physical route verification and potentially sourcing backup fiber from a different provider. If they need contractual five-nines with guaranteed physical diversity, they should be paying for it, and that payment should fund the actual physical verification work.

## Question 2
> **Design**: A regional SP wants to migrate from traditional transponder-based DWDM to IPoDWDM. They have 200km metro spans and 800km regional spans. What integration model would you recommend for each, and why?

### Answer

Different distances, different solutions. This is a textbook case for a hybrid approach.

**200 km metro spans → Full IPoDWDM with 400ZR+**

| Dimension | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Optics | QSFP-DD 400ZR+ (OpenZR+ spec) plugged directly into router line cards |
| Reach | 400ZR+ is rated for 400+ km with amplification — 200 km is comfortable with 10+ dB of margin |
| Line system | Open line system (OLS) — amplifiers + flex-grid ROADMs only. No standalone transponders. |
| Management | Router NMS manages the coherent optic. OpenConfig YANG models for optical PM. Single management domain. |
| Protection | TI-LFA at IP layer. No optical protection needed at these distances. |
| Cost | Eliminates transponder shelf entirely. ~30-40% capex reduction vs traditional architecture. |

At 200 km, this is an obvious win. ZR+ has massive margin, the optics are CMIS-managed through the router, and you eliminate an entire equipment layer plus its associated power, space, and operational burden.

**800 km regional spans → Multi-layer coordination with standalone transponders**

| Dimension | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Optics | Standalone coherent transponders (Ciena WaveLogic 6, Nokia PSE-6s, or equivalent) |
| Reach | 800 km requires optimized coherent performance — PCS-shaped DP-8QAM or DP-16QAM with Raman assist |
| Line system | Full DWDM line system with EDFAs + Raman amplification on longer spans |
| Management | Multi-layer orchestrator (Cisco Crosswork, Nokia NSP) correlating IP and optical topology |
| Protection | TI-LFA primary + optical restoration as secondary backup |
| Why not ZR+ | 800 km is at or beyond OpenZR+ reach limits. Standalone transponders have higher output power, better DSP performance, and dedicated thermal management. The ~2-3 dB performance advantage over pluggables is the difference between reliable service and marginal link budgets. |

At 800 km, the standalone transponder earns its keep. The performance premium (higher output power, dedicated DSP power budget, optimized thermal design) translates directly to link margin. A 400ZR+ pluggable running at the edge of its reach specification with zero margin is not something you want in production.

**Migration strategy:**

1. **Phase 1:** Deploy ZR+ on metro spans first. Immediate cost savings, lowest risk. Retire metro transponders as wavelengths are migrated.
2. **Phase 2:** Deploy multi-layer orchestrator to coordinate IP and optical on regional spans. This gives you unified visibility even while running traditional transponders.
3. **Phase 3:** As 800ZR+ pluggables arrive (~2027), evaluate whether they close the 800 km link budget with adequate margin. If yes, migrate regional spans to IPoDWDM. If no, maintain standalone transponders on regional and wait for next-gen pluggables.

**The key principle:** Don't force a single architecture across all distances. Match the integration model to the reach requirement. IPoDWDM where it works cleanly, standalone transponders where performance demands it, and a unified orchestration layer across both.

## Question 3
> **Troubleshooting**: A 400ZR optic shows pre-FEC BER gradually increasing from 1e-5 to 5e-4 over two weeks, with post-FEC BER still at zero. The far-end optic shows the same trend. What's your investigation plan?

### Answer

Gradual, symmetric degradation over two weeks with post-FEC still clean. Something in the path between them is slowly getting worse. The symmetry (both ends seeing it) rules out a unidirectional optic failure — this is the fiber plant or a shared component.

**Investigation plan, in order of likelihood:**

**1. Check connector cleanliness at both ends and any intermediate patch panels.**

This is the #1 cause of gradual optical degradation, and it's criminally underappreciated. Dust, oil (from fingers), and environmental contamination on fiber connectors cause scattering and back-reflection that worsen over time as particles settle or accumulate. A connector that was "clean enough" at install can degrade as the data center environment deposits particulates.

Action: Clean and inspect all connectors in the path with a fiber scope. If BER improves immediately after cleaning, you've found it.

**2. Pull OSNR and per-channel power trends from the line system.**

Compare OSNR readings at the receiver over the same two-week window. If OSNR is declining at the same rate as BER is increasing, the degradation is in the amplifier chain or fiber spans. If OSNR is stable but BER is increasing, the problem is in the coherent optic itself (DSP, laser, or photodetector aging).

**3. Check amplifier performance.**

EDFAs degrade over time — pump laser aging reduces gain, which the amplifier compensates by increasing pump current. Eventually pump current saturates and gain drops. Check pump laser bias current trends on all EDFAs in the path. An EDFA with steadily increasing pump current is telling you its pump laser is dying.

**4. Monitor the pluggable module temperature and laser bias current.**

ZR optics in QSFP-DD form factor run hot — thermal throttling is real. If the router's airflow has degraded (dirty filters, failed fan, adjacent slot generating more heat), the ZR module's internal temperature rises, DSP performance degrades, and laser output power drops. Check `show controllers optics` for module temperature trends.

Both ends showing the same trend might indicate a shared environmental cause — like a data hall HVAC change that's affecting both router enclosures.

**5. Check for new wavelength additions on the line system.**

If the optical team has been lighting new wavelengths over the past two weeks, each addition slightly increases nonlinear interference (XPM, SPM) and may have triggered EDFA power rebalancing. The result: your channel's per-channel power dropped slightly, OSNR degraded, and BER increased. This is a gradual, predictable phenomenon.

**6. OTDR the fiber spans if nothing above explains it.**

Fiber degradation is rare but real — water ingress into splice enclosures, cable strain from construction, or rodent damage to exposed cable can cause slowly worsening attenuation.

**Action threshold:** At 5e-4 pre-FEC BER with 400ZR's cFEC (concatenated FEC per the OIF 400ZR IA), you have significant coding gain margin remaining. You're not in danger today. But a two-week trend from 1e-5 to 5e-4 is a 50× increase — if it continues linearly in log space, you'll hit the FEC threshold in about 6-8 more weeks. Find and fix the root cause now, not when it's an outage.

## Question 4
> **Architecture**: Explain why SRLG-aware path computation is more critical in an IPoDWDM architecture than in a traditional transponder-based architecture.

### Answer

In a traditional architecture, the optical and IP layers are decoupled. The optical team provisions wavelengths and gives the router team "links." The IP routing protocol (IS-IS/OSPF) sees abstract point-to-point connections with no knowledge of the underlying fiber topology. If two IP links share fiber, the router doesn't know — but the optical layer has its own protection (1+1, ring switching, ROADM restoration) to handle fiber faults independently.

**In IPoDWDM, this safety net disappears.**

When you plug a coherent optic directly into a router, the router IS the transponder. There's no separate optical protection layer. The IP layer is the only protection layer. And here's the critical problem: **the IP routing protocol needs to know about physical fiber diversity to provide meaningful protection, because nobody else is going to do it.**

**Specific failure scenario without SRLG awareness:**

```
Router A ──── ZR+ (Path 1, fiber route X) ────── Router C
Router A ──── ZR+ (Path 2, fiber route X) ────── Router B ── Router C
```

Without SRLG information, OSPF/IS-IS just sees two independent paths from A to C. TI-LFA computes a backup through B. But both paths share fiber route X. A single fiber cut kills both the primary AND the backup. In a traditional architecture, the optical layer would have caught this — the optical team would never provision two "diverse" wavelengths on the same fiber route (or if they did, optical protection would reroute one).

**Why it's worse in IPoDWDM:**

1. **No optical protection fallback.** Traditional ROADMs can restore wavelengths to alternate fiber routes in 100-500ms. With IPoDWDM on an open line system (amplifiers only, no OTN switching), there's no optical-layer restoration. The coherent pluggable is either up or down — no automatic wavelength rerouting.

2. **More physical dependencies visible to IP.** In traditional architecture, the optical team abstracts physical complexity. The router team sees "Link A-C" without knowing it traverses 4 fiber spans, 3 amplifiers, and 2 ROADMs. In IPoDWDM, the router manages the coherent optic and can see optical PM data — but it still doesn't inherently know which links share fiber conduit.

3. **IP ECMP across shared fiber.** Without SRLG, a router might ECMP hash traffic across four 400G ZR links that all traverse the same fiber bundle. One backhoe takes out 1.6 Tbps of capacity simultaneously. With SRLG-aware TE, the controller ensures ECMP members use physically diverse fiber routes.

4. **SR-TE explicit paths need physical awareness.** When you compute an SR-TE policy with a disjoint backup, the constraint must include SRLG exclusion — not just node/link exclusion. Two links can be different links (different router interfaces, different wavelengths) and still share the same fiber trench.

**Implementation:** SRLG information must flow from the fiber plant (GIS/fiber management system) → optical NMS → IP layer. In IPoDWDM, this typically means:
- Manual SRLG assignment on router interfaces (tedious, error-prone)
- BGP-LS SRLG advertisement from a multi-layer controller
- OpenConfig `openconfig-network-instance` SRLG definitions populated by the orchestrator

The bottom line: IPoDWDM merges two layers into one, which simplifies operations in many ways — but it also means the IP layer inherits physical-layer diversity responsibilities that the optical layer used to handle. SRLG-aware path computation is not optional in this architecture.

## Question 5
> **Multi-layer**: An orchestrator reports that a 100G wavelength between two routers is running at 92% utilization. The routers show the link at only 35% utilization. What explains the discrepancy, and what would you do?

### Answer

Three possible explanations, and one of them is almost certainly the answer.

**Explanation 1 (most likely): The orchestrator is measuring the wrong layer.**

The "100G wavelength" is the optical channel (OTU4/ODU4 or raw DWDM λ). The router's "35% utilization" is the IP/Ethernet interface utilization. If there's an OTN muxponder between the router and the DWDM system, that 100G wavelength might be carrying traffic from *multiple* router ports — not just the one you're looking at.

Example: The muxponder aggregates 10 × 10GbE router ports onto one 100G wavelength. Each router port shows 35% utilization (3.5G each). The wavelength carries all 10 ports: 10 × 3.5G = 35G. At 35% wavelength utilization, not 92%.

But wait — the orchestrator says 92%. So maybe it's multiplexing traffic from *other sources too* — a separate muxponder shelf, a storage network, a video transport feed. The router team is only seeing their ports. The optical team's muxponder is carrying everyone's traffic.

**Explanation 2: OTN overhead and FEC overhead.**

A 100G wavelength (OTU4) has a line rate of 111.8 Gbps. The usable ODU4 payload is 104.8 Gbps. The 100GbE client maps into this with GFP/BMP adaptation. If the orchestrator measures raw line rate utilization (including FEC, OH, and any fixed-rate mapping overhead), and the router measures Ethernet payload utilization, the numbers will diverge. But this would give you maybe 10-15% discrepancy, not 35% vs 92%.

**Explanation 3: Counter/polling mismatch.**

The orchestrator and router poll at different intervals and use different averaging. If the orchestrator uses 1-second peak utilization and the router uses 5-minute average, bursty traffic shows as ~90% peak but ~35% average. This is especially common with video, storage replication, and backup traffic that comes in bursts.

**What I'd do:**

1. **Verify what the orchestrator is actually measuring.** Is it measuring the OTN/DWDM wavelength utilization (which may carry traffic from multiple sources) or specifically the router-to-router IP link?

2. **Check the multiplexing topology.** Is there a muxponder aggregating multiple clients onto this wavelength? If so, the router's 35% is only its share — others are consuming the rest.

3. **Synchronize polling intervals.** Compare both measurements at the same time granularity. Pull 1-second granularity from both the router and the optical NMS for the same 15-minute window.

4. **If it's truly a single router-to-router link with no muxponder:** Check for L2/L3 overhead inflation (VLAN tags, MPLS labels, GRE encap) that the router measures post-decap but the line system measures pre-decap. Also check for control plane traffic (BFD, IGP hellos, LDP) that the router doesn't count as "interface utilization" but still occupies line bandwidth.

**Most likely answer:** There's a muxponder or OTN switch between the routers that's aggregating traffic from multiple sources. The router only sees its portion. The orchestrator sees the full wavelength.
