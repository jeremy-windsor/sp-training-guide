## Question 1
> **Scenario**: You're deploying 400ZR between two DCs 95 km apart. The link comes up but pre-FEC BER is 8×10⁻³ — above your 3 dB margin target. What are your options, and which do you recommend?

### Answer

400ZR uses DP-16QAM at ~60 GBaud with oFEC (open FEC, threshold ~1.25 × 10⁻²). Your pre-FEC BER of 8 × 10⁻³ means the link is *functional* — you're below the FEC threshold — but you only have about 1.5–2 dB of margin instead of the 3 dB you need. At 95 km unamplified, you're near the edge of 400ZR's rated reach (~120 km max).

**Options:**

**1. Clean and verify all connectors and patch cords.** 
Cost: $0. Time: 1 hour. Impact: potentially 1-3 dB improvement.

This is always step one. Dirty connectors are the single biggest source of avoidable optical penalty. Inspect every connector with a fiber scope. Any contamination = clean and retest. A single dirty connector can cost you 1-2 dB. If this fixes your margin problem, congratulations — you just saved $50K+ in equipment.

**2. Reduce connector/patch cord count.**
Eliminate any unnecessary patch panels between the ZR optic and the fiber plant. Every connector pair adds ~0.3-0.5 dB of insertion loss. If you have 4 patch panels (data center structured cabling), that's 1.2-2 dB of avoidable loss.

**3. Add a single EDFA (in-line amplifier or booster).**
Cost: ~$5-15K. Impact: 15-20 dB of gain, completely changes the link budget.

Deploy an EDFA booster at the transmit end or a pre-amp at the receive end. This transforms a marginal unamplified link into a comfortable amplified one. At 95 km with ~19 dB span loss (0.2 dB/km), an EDFA with +17 dBm output easily closes the link with 10+ dB margin. This is the standard solution for ZR links in the 80-120 km range.

**4. Switch to OpenZR+ / CFP2-DCO.**
Cost: $10-30K more per pair. Impact: 2-5 dB better performance.

OpenZR+ pluggables typically have higher output power (+1 to +3 dBm vs ZR's -10 to 0 dBm), better Rx sensitivity, and vendor-optimized FEC. A CFP2-DCO in particular has dedicated thermal management and can push 3-5 dB better performance than a QSFP-DD ZR. At 95 km, this likely provides the margin you need without any line-system changes.

**5. Drop to 200G (DP-QPSK on the same optic, if supported).**
Cost: $0 (software change). Impact: ~6 dB OSNR improvement.

Some 400ZR optics support rate reduction to 200G DP-QPSK. The required OSNR drops from ~18 dB to ~12 dB, giving you massive margin. But you lose half your capacity. This is a last resort unless you genuinely don't need 400G.

**My recommendation:** Option 1 first (always), then Option 3 if connectors aren't the issue. A single EDFA is cheap, simple, and gives you abundant margin for future degradation. It's a much better investment than swapping optics (Option 4) or sacrificing capacity (Option 5). If this is a DCI application, you'll likely want to grow capacity anyway — an amplified link supports future wavelength additions.

## Question 2
> **Design**: A customer wants to upgrade from 96×100G (50 GHz grid, 9.6 Tb/s) to maximum C-band capacity. They're considering 64×400G (75 GHz) vs 32×800G (150 GHz). What's the total capacity of each option? What factors beyond raw bandwidth should influence the decision?

### Answer

**Raw capacity math:**

| Option | Channels | Per-Channel | Total | Grid |
|--------|----------|-------------|-------|------|
| Current | 96 × 100G | 100G (DP-QPSK) | 9.6 Tbps | 50 GHz fixed |
| Option A | 64 × 400G | 400G (DP-16QAM, ~64 GBd) | 25.6 Tbps | 75 GHz flex |
| Option B | 32 × 800G | 800G (DP-16QAM, ~130 GBd or 2×65 GBd) | 25.6 Tbps | 150 GHz flex |

Same total capacity. Identical. So the decision comes down to factors beyond raw throughput:

**Factors favoring 64 × 400G:**

1. **Mature, proven technology.** 400G coherent (WaveLogic 5e, PSE-Vs, ICE6) has been in production since 2022. You're deploying well-understood, well-tested hardware. 800G is generation-current (2025-2026) with less field history.

2. **Better reach at 400G.** At DP-16QAM, 400G at 64 GBd has better OSNR tolerance than 800G at 130 GBd. For long-haul routes (>1,000 km), 400G gives you more margin. 800G may require DP-8QAM at those distances, which means it's actually delivering 600G per channel, not 800G.

3. **Finer traffic granularity.** 64 individual wavelengths vs 32. More wavelengths = more flexibility to route individual services to different destinations via ROADMs. If you have 40 unique destination pairs, 64 wavelengths maps better than 32.

4. **Failure blast radius.** One 400G wavelength failure affects 400G of traffic. One 800G failure affects 800G. If your service protection is at the wavelength level, smaller wavelengths = smaller impact per failure.

5. **Cost today.** 400G transponders and ZR+ pluggables are commodity-priced. 800G is premium-priced with fewer vendor options.

**Factors favoring 32 × 800G:**

1. **Half the transponder count.** 32 transponder pairs instead of 64. Less rack space, less power, fewer spares, fewer management objects. Operational simplicity matters at scale.

2. **Power efficiency.** 800G transponders deliver ~2× capacity per watt compared to 400G. In a power-constrained facility, this is significant. A 25.6 Tbps system with 32 transponders uses roughly 30-40% less power than 64 transponders.

3. **Future headroom.** The 150 GHz flex-grid allocation positions you for 1.6 Tbps per wavelength when next-gen DSPs arrive (~2028). The 75 GHz slots won't accommodate 1.6T without re-gridding.

4. **Spectral efficiency per channel.** 800G at 150 GHz = 5.33 bit/s/Hz. 400G at 75 GHz = 5.33 bit/s/Hz. Identical spectral efficiency — but the 800G system has more "headroom" per slot for PCS rate adaptation. If a route is slightly too long for 800G, PCS can smoothly back off to 700G or 600G without wasting the 150 GHz slot entirely.

5. **ROADM port density.** Fewer channels means fewer WSS ports consumed per ROADM node. On a multi-degree ROADM (8+ degrees), port count can become a constraint.

**What I'd recommend:** It depends on reach.

- **Metro/DCI (<500 km):** 32 × 800G. Lower transponder count, lower power, OSNR margin is comfortable at this distance.
- **Regional/long-haul (>1,000 km):** 64 × 400G. The reach advantage of 400G at DP-16QAM is real. 800G would need to drop to DP-8QAM, making the actual per-channel capacity ~600G, which changes the total capacity math entirely.
- **Mixed distance network:** Deploy both. 800G on short spans, 400G on long spans, flex-grid ROADMs accommodating both slot widths. This is what most SPs will actually do.

## Question 3
> **Troubleshooting**: A coherent link has been running clean for 6 months. Today, the pre-FEC BER jumped from 10⁻⁵ to 5×10⁻³ and CD readings changed from 320 ps/nm to 1,840 ps/nm. Post-FEC is still clean. What happened? What's the risk?

### Answer

The CD jump from 320 ps/nm to 1,840 ps/nm — a 1,520 ps/nm increase — tells the whole story. **Someone rerouted the fiber.**

**What happened:**

Chromatic dispersion accumulates linearly with fiber length: ~17 ps/(nm·km) for standard G.652 fiber in the C-band. A jump of 1,520 ps/nm corresponds to roughly 89 km of additional fiber in the path.

```
ΔCD = 1,840 - 320 = 1,520 ps/nm
Additional fiber ≈ 1,520 / 17 = ~89 km
```

Possible causes:
1. **Fiber maintenance reroute.** The fiber provider rerouted your circuit to a longer path (perhaps a diverse route during cable repair). They may or may not have told you.
2. **ROADM wavelength reroute.** An optical network controller switched your wavelength to a longer optical path — possibly in response to an EDFA failure, a fiber cut on the shorter path, or an optimization run.
3. **Patch panel error.** During maintenance, a technician connected your wavelength through a DCF (dispersion compensation fiber) spool or an unintended loop-back that adds path length.

**The coherent DSP is handling it** — that's why post-FEC is still clean. Modern coherent DSP can compensate ±100,000 ps/nm of CD digitally. 1,840 ps/nm is well within the compensation window. But the BER jumped because the longer path has more span loss, more ASE noise, and worse OSNR. The DSP compensates the CD, but it can't compensate the SNR degradation from the additional fiber and amplifiers.

**The risk:**

Your margin just got eaten. Going from 1e-5 to 5e-3 pre-FEC BER represents roughly 8-10 dB of OSNR degradation. With oFEC threshold at ~1.25e-2, you now have about 3-4 dB of margin instead of the 13+ dB you had before.

If this is a temporary reroute (fiber repair), the risk is moderate — the link is functional and will return to normal when the fiber is restored.

If this is a permanent reroute, you need to:
1. **Confirm with the fiber provider** that the path change is intentional and whether the original path will be restored.
2. **Re-run the link budget** for the new path length. At 89 km longer, you may need additional amplification.
3. **Monitor for further degradation.** The longer path may traverse older fiber with higher PMD or pass through more ROADM nodes with filter narrowing effects.
4. **Update your SRLG database** — the physical route has changed, which means your diversity assumptions may be invalid.

**Immediate action:** Call the optical team / fiber provider and ask "did you reroute our circuit?" If the answer is "yes, temporarily for maintenance," document it and monitor. If the answer is "no," you have a bigger problem — either a ROADM made an unauthorized path change, or someone physically moved your fiber without authorization.

## Question 4
> **Architecture**: When would you choose a CFP2-DCO in a router slot over a QSFP-DD 400ZR, even though both are "pluggable coherent"? Name three scenarios.

### Answer

Both are pluggable. Both go in a router faceplate. But they're very different beasts. CFP2-DCO is the professional tool; QSFP-DD 400ZR is the consumer product. Here's when you pay the premium for CFP2-DCO:

**Scenario 1: Extended reach (300–1,000+ km)**

400ZR is specified for point-to-point links up to ~120 km unamplified, and with amplification can stretch to maybe 200–300 km before running out of margin. CFP2-DCO modules support full line-system grade performance:

- Higher output power: +1 to +3 dBm (vs 400ZR's -10 to 0 dBm)
- Better receiver sensitivity: 2-3 dB better minimum OSNR
- Advanced FEC options: vendor-specific soft-decision FEC with higher coding gain than oFEC
- PCS for continuous rate adaptation (200G to 400G based on path)

For a 600 km regional backbone link, 400ZR won't close the link budget. CFP2-DCO with DP-8QAM + PCS will deliver 300-400G reliably. The alternative is a standalone transponder, which eliminates the "pluggable in the router" advantage entirely.

**Scenario 2: Multi-carrier/super-channel for 800G in a single slot**

Some CFP2-DCO implementations support dual-carrier operation — two 400G sub-carriers from a single module, delivering 800G per slot. QSFP-DD 400ZR is strictly single-carrier, single-wavelength, 400G max.

If you need 800G per router port but don't want to burn two QSFP-DD slots (and their associated host-side bandwidth), a single CFP2-DCO at 800G is more port-efficient. This matters on platforms where faceplate density is the bottleneck.

**Scenario 3: Deployment on heavily loaded DWDM line systems with tight OSNR margins**

In a dense metro/regional DWDM system with 80+ channels, high channel loading, and multiple ROADM pass-throughs, every dB of transponder performance matters. CFP2-DCO advantages in this environment:

- Higher output power enables better per-channel OSNR after ROADM cascade penalties
- Better Tx spectral purity (narrower line-width laser, tighter Nyquist shaping) reduces inter-channel interference
- Vendor-optimized nonlinear compensation algorithms (not available in 400ZR's interop-constrained DSP)
- Temperature-controlled packaging prevents the thermal derating that QSFP-DD modules suffer in high-density router chassis

400ZR was designed for the "easy" use case — short-reach DCI with few or no intermediate ROADMs. When you cascade through 5+ ROADMs on a loaded system, the filter narrowing penalty, PDL accumulation, and OSNR degradation compound in ways that expose 400ZR's performance limitations. CFP2-DCO's extra power budget and DSP headroom handle this gracefully.

**Bonus scenario:** When your router platform has CFP2 slots but limited QSFP-DD slots (common on older generation line cards designed before 400ZR existed). Platform constraints trump theoretical preferences.

## Question 5
> **Operations**: Explain the FEC cliff to a junior engineer. Why is it dangerous, and what operational practices prevent you from falling off it?

### Answer

Imagine you're driving in fog. FEC is your fog lights — they correct your visibility enough to stay on the road. As the fog gets thicker (BER increases), the lights work harder. At some fog density, they're working at maximum capacity but you can still see. One tiny bit thicker — and suddenly you see *nothing*. That's the FEC cliff.

**The technical reality:**

FEC (Forward Error Correction) adds redundant check bits to transmitted data. The receiver uses these to detect and correct bit errors. FEC can correct errors up to a specific rate — the **FEC threshold** (also called the pre-FEC BER limit). Below the threshold, post-FEC BER is essentially zero (< 10⁻¹⁵). Above the threshold, post-FEC BER goes from zero to catastrophic in a tiny BER increment.

```
Post-FEC BER
     │
10⁻⁵ │                                          ╱
     │                                         ╱
10⁻⁹ │                                        ╱
     │                                       ╱   ← THE CLIFF
10⁻¹³│────────────────────────────────────╱
     │  ← "error-free" region             │
10⁻¹⁵│────────────────────────────────────┤
     └────────────────────────────────────┴──── Pre-FEC BER
                                     FEC threshold
                                     (~1.25e-2 for oFEC)
```

The transition from "error-free" to "total failure" happens over a pre-FEC BER change of less than one order of magnitude. There's no graceful degradation. No warning. No "a few errors getting through." It's either perfect or broken.

**Why it's dangerous:**

1. **False sense of security.** Post-FEC BER has been zero for 3 years. The monitoring dashboard shows green. Nobody checks pre-FEC BER. Meanwhile, pre-FEC BER has been slowly creeping from 1e-6 toward 1e-2 due to amplifier aging, connector contamination, or fiber degradation. One day it crosses the threshold and the link goes from 100% clean to 100% down with no intermediate warning.

2. **Non-linear failure mode.** Most network failures are gradual — you see increasing errors, packet loss, or latency before a complete outage. The FEC cliff violates this expectation. It creates a scenario where a 0.5 dB change in OSNR (from a temperature swing, a new wavelength being added, or a connector bump) takes you from perfect to dead.

3. **Thermal and environmental sensitivity.** Optical performance varies with temperature, polarization state, and vibration. A link that has 1 dB of FEC margin in summer may have 0 dB of margin in winter (or vice versa, depending on the failure mode). The cliff turns normal environmental variation into binary outage events.

**Operational practices to stay safe:**

| Practice | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Monitor pre-FEC BER, not just post-FEC** | Set alarms at 50% and 75% of the FEC threshold. If threshold is 1.25e-2, alarm at 6e-3 (critical) and 3e-3 (warning). |
| **Maintain minimum system margin** | Design for ≥3 dB margin above the FEC threshold at end-of-life conditions. This accounts for aging, temperature, and future wavelength additions. |
| **Pre-FEC BER trending** | Track pre-FEC BER monthly. Any upward trend triggers investigation before it becomes an outage. A graph that slopes up is a prediction of failure. |
| **Post-maintenance verification** | After ANY fiber work (splicing, patching, cleaning), verify pre-FEC BER returned to baseline. Don't just check "link up." |
| **Temperature monitoring** | Correlate optical PM with ambient temperature. Know your worst-case seasonal margin. |
| **Proactive connector cleaning** | Schedule annual connector inspection and cleaning on all optical patch panels. Don't wait for degradation. |
| **Capacity planning margin** | When adding new wavelengths to a DWDM system, model the OSNR impact on existing channels. Don't fill the system to 100% — the last 10% of channels degrade everyone. |

**The one-liner for your junior engineer:** "FEC is not a margin. FEC is a cliff with a fence. Your job is to make sure nobody builds a parking lot near the fence."
