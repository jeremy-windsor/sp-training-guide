# 9.2 — DWDM Fundamentals — Protocol Theory

## Protocol Overview

Dense Wavelength Division Multiplexing (DWDM) is the technology that turned fiber optic cables from single-channel pipes into multi-terabit highways. The principle is straightforward: multiple optical signals at different wavelengths (colors of light) travel simultaneously through a single fiber. Each wavelength carries an independent data stream. A fiber that once carried one 10G signal now carries 96 or more signals at 100G+ each — the same glass, orders of magnitude more capacity.

DWDM emerged in the mid-1990s when the internet's exponential growth made laying new fiber economically impractical. The alternative: pack more signals into existing fiber using wavelength multiplexing. ITU-T G.694.1 standardized the DWDM frequency grid, and the technology rapidly became the foundation of all long-haul and metro optical networks.

The "Dense" in DWDM refers to channel spacing. Coarse WDM (CWDM, ITU-T G.694.2) uses 20nm spacing — wide gaps, few channels (up to 18), low cost, short reach. DWDM uses 100GHz (0.8nm) or 50GHz (0.4nm) spacing — tight gaps, many channels (40-96+), higher cost, long reach. Modern flexible grid systems (ITU-T G.694.1 2012 revision) support 12.5GHz granularity for even denser packing.

## Core Mechanisms

### 1. Wavelength Physics

**Light as carrier**: Optical signals in DWDM systems operate in the infrared spectrum, primarily in the C-band (1530-1565nm) and sometimes the L-band (1565-1625nm). These bands were chosen because they align with the lowest attenuation window of silica fiber and the gain bandwidth of Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifiers (EDFAs).

**ITU-T frequency grid (G.694.1):**
- Reference frequency: 193.1 THz (1552.52nm)
- Channel spacing options: 100GHz, 50GHz, 25GHz, 12.5GHz
- C-band with 50GHz spacing: ~96 channels
- C-band with 100GHz spacing: ~48 channels

**Wavelength vs frequency**: The industry uses both interchangeably. Wavelength (nm) and frequency (THz) are inversely related: λ = c/f. Higher frequency = shorter wavelength. Channel numbering typically uses frequency (193.1 THz, 193.15 THz, etc.).

**Fiber attenuation**: Standard single-mode fiber (G.652) has approximately 0.2 dB/km attenuation in the C-band. Over 100km, the signal loses 20dB — about 99% of its power. This is why amplification is essential.

**Chromatic dispersion**: Different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds through fiber. Over long distances, this spreads the signal pulse, causing intersymbol interference. Standard SMF has about 17 ps/(nm·km) dispersion in the C-band. At 10G, dispersion limits reach to ~80km on standard fiber. At 100G, it's ~2km without compensation. Modern coherent DSP compensates electronically.

**Polarization Mode Dispersion (PMD)**: The two polarization states of light travel at slightly different speeds due to fiber imperfections. PMD is stochastic — it varies with temperature, vibration, and fiber stress. Modern coherent receivers compensate for PMD electronically.

### 2. EDFA — Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier

The EDFA is the enabling technology for DWDM. It amplifies all wavelengths in the C-band (or L-band) simultaneously without converting to electrical signals.

**How it works:**
1. A short length of fiber is doped with Erbium ions (Er³⁺).
2. A pump laser (typically 980nm or 1480nm) excites the Erbium ions to a higher energy state (population inversion).
3. Incoming optical signals stimulate the excited ions to emit photons at the same wavelength, phase, and direction — stimulated emission.
4. The signal exits amplified. All C-band wavelengths are amplified simultaneously.

**Key EDFA parameters:**

**Gain**: Typically 15-30dB. A 20dB gain amplifies the signal by 100x. Gain is not flat across the C-band — the Erbium gain profile peaks around 1530nm and 1550nm. Gain Flattening Filters (GFFs) are used to equalize gain across the band.

**Noise Figure (NF)**: Every amplifier adds noise. The noise figure quantifies this — typically 4-7dB for an EDFA. Lower is better. Noise accumulates at each amplifier in a chain, degrading the signal-to-noise ratio (OSNR). The OSNR at the receiver determines whether the signal can be decoded.

**OSNR budget**: For a chain of N amplifiers, the OSNR degrades approximately as:
- OSNR depends on accumulated ASE from each amplifier (output power, gain, NF, reference bandwidth). Simplified formulas like `58 + P_in - NF - 10·log10(N)` appear in vendor training but omit gain/output power terms — use vendor link-planning tools for real designs.
- Where P_in is the per-channel launch power in dBm

**Saturation**: EDFAs have a maximum output power (typically 17-23 dBm total). As channels are added, the per-channel power decreases. Channel loading (number of active wavelengths) directly affects per-channel power and OSNR.

### 3. Raman Amplification

Raman amplification uses the fiber itself as the gain medium, complementing EDFAs.

**How it works:**
1. A high-power pump laser (typically 1420-1480nm for C-band amplification) is launched into the fiber, usually counter-propagating (backward Raman).
2. The pump photons interact with the silica molecules via stimulated Raman scattering.
3. Energy transfers from pump photons to signal photons — the signal is amplified.
4. The gain peak occurs approximately 13 THz (100nm) below the pump frequency.

**Distributed vs lumped**: Raman amplification is distributed — it happens along the entire fiber span, not at a single point. This means the signal never drops as low as it would without Raman, resulting in better OSNR than EDFA-only systems.

**Hybrid EDFA + Raman**: Most modern long-haul systems use both. Raman provides distributed gain in the fiber (improving OSNR), and EDFAs provide lumped gain at amplifier sites. The combination extends reach by 30-50% compared to EDFA alone.

### 4. ROADM — Reconfigurable Optical Add-Drop Multiplexer

ROADMs are the switching fabric of the optical layer. They selectively add, drop, or pass-through individual wavelengths at a node without converting to electrical signals.

**ROADM architecture evolution:**

**First generation — Fixed OADM**: Specific wavelengths were hardwired for add/drop at each node. Changing which wavelengths were dropped required physical fiber patching. Not reconfigurable.

**Second generation — Wavelength Blocker (WB) ROADM**: Could block or pass any wavelength on a per-channel basis. Add/drop was still limited to fixed ports. Partially reconfigurable.

**Third generation — Wavelength Selective Switch (WSS) ROADM**: The modern standard. A WSS can independently route any wavelength from any input port to any output port. Fully reconfigurable. Software-controlled.

**CDC-F ROADM (Colorless, Directionless, Contentionless, Flex-grid):**
- **Colorless**: Any wavelength can be added/dropped on any port. No wavelength-specific hardware.
- **Directionless**: A wavelength can be routed to any fiber direction without manual patching.
- **Contentionless**: Multiple signals on the same wavelength (from different directions) can coexist at the node without blocking.
- **Flex-grid**: Supports variable channel widths (not locked to 50/100GHz grid). Enables super-channels and optimal spectrum utilization.

**WSS technology**: Most modern WSS units use Liquid Crystal on Silicon (LCoS) technology — a spatial light modulator that independently steers each wavelength. Typical WSS supports 1x9 or 1x20 port counts with 96+ channel granularity.

**ROADM degree**: The "degree" of a ROADM is the number of fiber directions it supports. A 4-degree ROADM connects to 4 fiber pairs (8 fibers — 4 transmit, 4 receive). Each degree has a WSS for independent wavelength routing.

### 5. Optical Signal-to-Noise Ratio (OSNR)

OSNR is the primary metric for optical signal quality in DWDM systems.

**Definition**: The ratio of signal power to noise power within a reference bandwidth (typically 0.1nm or 12.5GHz).

**Why OSNR matters**: Every amplifier in the chain adds Amplified Spontaneous Emission (ASE) noise. After many amplifiers, the accumulated noise can overwhelm the signal. The receiver needs a minimum OSNR to decode correctly — the required OSNR depends on the modulation format and baud rate.

**Typical required OSNR values:**
- 10G NRZ (on-off keying): ~12dB
- 100G DP-QPSK: ~14dB
- 400G DP-16QAM: ~22dB
- 800G DP-64QAM: ~30dB+

Higher-order modulation packs more bits per symbol but requires exponentially better OSNR. This is the fundamental trade-off in optical networking: capacity vs reach.

### 6. Optical Spectrum Management

**Channel plan**: The allocation of wavelengths to services. In fixed-grid systems, channels are assigned on the 50GHz or 100GHz grid. In flex-grid systems, channels can be any width in 12.5GHz increments.

**Super-channels**: Multiple optical carriers bonded together into a single logical channel, sharing a flex-grid spectral slot wider than a single channel. Example: a 400G super-channel might use 2 × 200G carriers in a 75GHz slot.

**Spectrum fragmentation**: As channels are added and removed over time, the available spectrum becomes fragmented — free slots are scattered rather than contiguous. This limits the ability to provision wide super-channels. Defragmentation (hitless wavelength shifting) addresses this but is operationally complex.

## Protocol Interactions

**DWDM ↔ OTN**: OTN (Section 9.3) provides the digital framing layer that rides on DWDM wavelengths. Each wavelength carries an OTU frame. DWDM provides the physical transport; OTN provides the digital wrapper.

**DWDM ↔ Coherent optics**: Modern coherent transponders (Section 9.5) generate the modulated optical signals that DWDM carries. The transponder determines the modulation format, baud rate, and required spectral width. The DWDM system provides the amplified optical path.

**DWDM ↔ IP/MPLS**: Router interfaces connect to DWDM transponders (either as pluggable optics or external muxponders). The router sees an Ethernet or OTN interface; the DWDM system sees a wavelength. The gap between these two views is where packet-optical integration (Section 9.4) lives.

## Edge Cases & Gotchas

**1. EDFA gain tilt under channel loading changes**: Adding or removing wavelengths changes the EDFA gain profile. Without automatic gain control, remaining channels can experience power excursions — some get too much power (nonlinear effects), others too little (OSNR degradation).

**2. Four-wave mixing on zero-dispersion fiber**: On fibers with near-zero dispersion at the signal wavelength (like G.653 DSF), four-wave mixing generates crosstalk between channels. This is why NZ-DSF (G.655) was developed — a small amount of dispersion suppresses FWM.

**3. ROADM cascade OSNR penalty**: Each ROADM pass-through adds insertion loss (typically 8-12dB) and requires amplification. After 5-6 ROADM hops, the accumulated OSNR penalty may require signal regeneration (O-E-O conversion).

**4. Flex-grid and legacy fixed-grid interop**: Mixing flex-grid and fixed-grid equipment on the same fiber requires careful spectrum planning. A flex-grid super-channel may not fit in the slots that fixed-grid ROADMs can process.

**5. Fiber nonlinearities at high launch power**: Increasing per-channel launch power improves OSNR but introduces nonlinear effects (self-phase modulation, cross-phase modulation, stimulated Brillouin scattering). There's an optimal launch power per channel — above it, nonlinearities degrade performance faster than the OSNR improves.
