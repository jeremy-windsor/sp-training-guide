# 9.5 — Coherent Optics — Protocol Theory

## Protocol Overview

Coherent optics is the technology that broke through the capacity ceiling of traditional intensity-modulated direct-detect (IM-DD) optical systems. In IM-DD, information is encoded in the intensity (brightness) of the light — on or off, bright or dim. That's one dimension of information. Coherent optics uses four dimensions: amplitude, phase, and two polarization states. This multiplies the bits per symbol dramatically, enabling 100G, 400G, and 800G+ per wavelength on the same fiber that once carried 10G.

The "coherent" in coherent optics refers to the use of a local oscillator laser at the receiver. Instead of simply measuring light intensity (direct detection), the receiver combines the incoming signal with a local laser of known frequency and phase, then measures the interference pattern. This interference — or "beating" — extracts both amplitude and phase information from the signal, enabling advanced modulation formats.

Coherent detection was well understood in theory for decades but impractical until high-speed Digital Signal Processing (DSP) became available in the late 2000s. The DSP handles chromatic dispersion compensation, PMD compensation, carrier frequency offset estimation, and phase recovery — all in real-time, at line rate. The combination of coherent detection and DSP is what made 100G commercially viable around 2010, and it's been scaling ever since.

## Core Mechanisms

### 1. Modulation Formats

Coherent systems encode data by modulating the electric field of light across multiple dimensions.

**Constellation diagrams**: Each modulation format has a constellation diagram — a 2D plot where the X-axis is the In-phase (I) component and the Y-axis is the Quadrature (Q) component. Each point in the constellation represents a unique symbol (combination of amplitude and phase).

**Key modulation formats:**

**QPSK (Quadrature Phase-Shift Keying):**
- 4 constellation points
- 2 bits per symbol per polarization
- 4 bits per symbol total (dual-polarization = DP-QPSK)
- Most robust: points are far apart, tolerates more noise
- Used for long-haul (1000km+) and submarine systems
- 100G at 32 GBaud: 32 × 4 = 128 Gbps gross (100G net after FEC overhead)

**8QAM (8-state Quadrature Amplitude Modulation):**
- 8 constellation points
- 3 bits per symbol per polarization
- 6 bits per symbol total (DP-8QAM)
- Intermediate robustness and capacity
- Used for metro-regional (300-600km)
- 300G at 50 GBaud or similar combinations

**16QAM:**
- 16 constellation points
- 4 bits per symbol per polarization
- 8 bits per symbol total (DP-16QAM)
- Less robust: points are closer together, needs better OSNR
- Used for metro and DCI (80-300km)
- 400G at 50 GBaud or 200G at 32 GBaud

**64QAM:**
- 64 constellation points
- 6 bits per symbol per polarization
- 12 bits per symbol total (DP-64QAM)
- Least robust: requires excellent OSNR and very clean optical path
- Used for short-reach DCI (0-80km)
- 800G at ~65 GBaud

**The trade-off is always the same**: Higher-order modulation = more bits per symbol = higher capacity per wavelength, BUT requires better OSNR = shorter reach. Physics doesn't negotiate.

### 2. Dual Polarization

Single-mode fiber supports two orthogonal polarization states of light. Coherent systems transmit independent data on each polarization, doubling capacity.

**How it works:**
1. Transmitter splits the data stream into two halves.
2. Each half modulates a separate polarization (X-pol and Y-pol) using a dual-polarization IQ modulator.
3. Both polarizations are combined and launched into the fiber.
4. At the receiver, a polarization beam splitter separates the two polarizations.
5. Each polarization is detected independently by the coherent receiver.
6. DSP demultiplexes and recovers both data streams.

**Polarization rotation in fiber**: As the signal travels through fiber, the polarization states rotate unpredictably (due to birefringence). The receiver's DSP must continuously track and compensate for this rotation. This is one of the DSP's most computationally intensive tasks.

### 3. Coherent Receiver Architecture

**Components:**
1. **Local Oscillator (LO) laser**: A narrow-linewidth laser at (approximately) the same frequency as the transmitter. Provides the phase reference.
2. **90° optical hybrid**: Combines the incoming signal with the LO in four combinations (I and Q for each polarization). Produces four interference patterns.
3. **Balanced photodetectors**: Convert the four optical interference patterns into four electrical signals. Balanced detection cancels common-mode noise.
4. **ADC (Analog-to-Digital Converters)**: Digitize the four electrical signals at the baud rate (or higher). Modern ADCs run at 64+ GSa/s with 8+ bit resolution.
5. **DSP ASIC**: Processes the digitized signals to recover the data.

**Why balanced detection**: Each optical hybrid output is a pair of complementary signals. Subtracting them (balanced detection) doubles the signal and cancels the DC component and common-mode laser noise. Without balanced detection, signal quality degrades significantly.

### 4. DSP Pipeline

The DSP is the brain of the coherent receiver. It performs multiple signal recovery functions in a fixed processing pipeline.

**Typical DSP pipeline stages (in order):**

**Stage 1 — Chromatic Dispersion Compensation:**
- Applies a digital filter that reverses the fiber's chromatic dispersion.
- The filter is computed from the known dispersion coefficient and fiber length.
- Can compensate thousands of km of dispersion without optical DCMs (dispersion compensation modules).
- This single DSP function eliminated the need for optical dispersion maps that plagued 10G systems.

**Stage 2 — Clock Recovery:**
- Recovers the symbol timing from the received signal.
- Determines the optimal sampling instant for each symbol.

**Stage 3 — Polarization Demultiplexing and PMD Compensation:**
- Uses an adaptive equalizer (typically a butterfly FIR filter).
- Separates the X and Y polarization tributaries.
- Compensates for PMD (polarization mode dispersion) and residual chromatic dispersion.
- Continuously adapts to changing fiber conditions.

**Stage 4 — Carrier Frequency Offset Estimation:**
- The transmitter laser and LO laser are never at exactly the same frequency.
- DSP estimates the frequency offset and corrects it digitally.
- Typical offset: up to several hundred MHz.

**Stage 5 — Carrier Phase Recovery:**
- Estimates and corrects for phase noise from both the transmitter and LO lasers.
- Uses algorithms like Viterbi-Viterbi (for QPSK) or blind phase search (for higher-order QAM).
- Phase noise tolerance determines the laser linewidth requirements.

**Stage 6 — Symbol Decision and FEC Decoding:**
- Maps the corrected constellation points to the nearest valid symbol (hard or soft decision).
- Soft-decision FEC decodes the bit stream, correcting remaining errors.
- Output: clean data stream at the client rate.

### 5. Probabilistic Constellation Shaping (PCS)

PCS is a technique that optimizes capacity by making the modulation format adaptable to the channel conditions.

**The insight**: In a standard QAM constellation, all symbols are used equally often. But symbols at the outer edge of the constellation (high amplitude) are more susceptible to noise. If you use high-amplitude symbols less frequently and low-amplitude symbols more frequently, the average signal power decreases while maintaining the same information rate — effectively improving OSNR efficiency.

**How PCS works:**
1. Data bits are passed through a Distribution Matcher that maps uniform data into shaped symbol sequences (non-uniform probability distribution).
2. The shaped symbols preferentially use inner constellation points (lower amplitude).
3. The receiver's Distribution Dematcher reverses the shaping.
4. Net result: ~1dB SNR gain (equivalent to ~15% more reach) at no extra bandwidth cost.

**Why PCS matters operationally**: PCS enables rate-flexible transponders. Instead of switching between discrete modulation formats (QPSK → 8QAM → 16QAM), a PCS transponder can dial any capacity between QPSK and 64QAM in fine increments (e.g., 25G steps from 100G to 800G). The modulation format is fixed (typically 64QAM), but the shaping ratio varies.

### 6. Shannon Limit

The Shannon-Hartley theorem defines the theoretical maximum information capacity of a communication channel:

**C = B × log₂(1 + SNR)**

Where C = capacity (bits/second), B = bandwidth (Hz), SNR = signal-to-noise ratio (linear).

**What this means for optics**: Given a fixed bandwidth (determined by baud rate and spectral efficiency) and SNR (determined by OSNR, modulation, and fiber impairments), there's a hard ceiling on capacity. Modern coherent systems with SD-FEC and PCS operate within 1-2dB of the Shannon limit. We're approaching fundamental physics limits.

**Spectral efficiency**: Shannon capacity per unit bandwidth (bits/second/Hz). Current commercial systems achieve 5-8 b/s/Hz. The Shannon limit for typical optical channels is ~10-12 b/s/Hz. Not much room left.

**Beyond Shannon**: Multi-band transmission (C+L band, eventually S-band), multi-core fiber, multi-mode approaches, and new fiber types are the paths to capacity growth once Shannon-per-wavelength is exhausted.

### 7. Pluggable Coherent Optics

The industry trend: moving coherent DSP from external transponder boxes into pluggable modules that go directly into router/switch ports.

**Form factor evolution:**
- **CFP/CFP2**: Early coherent pluggables. Large form factor. 100G.
- **CFP2-DCO (Digital Coherent Optic)**: Coherent DSP inside a CFP2 module. 100G-400G.
- **QSFP-DD / OSFP**: Modern compact form factor. 400G coherent in a pluggable the size of a regular QSFP.
- **QSFP-DD800**: Next generation. 800G coherent pluggable.

**Why this matters**: Pluggable coherent optics collapse the transponder into the router. The router's QSFP-DD port connects directly to the DWDM line system — no external muxponder. This simplifies the network architecture (IP-over-DWDM without separate OTN layer) and reduces cost, power, and space.

**ZR and ZR+**: 
- **400ZR (OIF standard)**: 400G DP-16QAM at ~60 GBaud, target reach 80-120km, designed for DCI. Interoperable across vendors.
- **OpenZR+**: Extended reach (up to 2000km) with flexible modulation (QPSK to 16QAM), higher launch power, and multiple rates (100G-400G). Multi-vendor through OpenROADM MSA.

## Protocol Interactions

**Coherent optics ↔ DWDM**: Coherent transponders generate the modulated optical signal; DWDM amplifies and routes it. The transponder's baud rate and modulation format determine the required spectral width. The DWDM system's OSNR and reach determine which modulation formats are viable.

**Coherent optics ↔ OTN**: Traditional transponders use OTN framing (OTU4, OTUCn) to wrap client signals before coherent modulation. 400ZR is an OIF-defined 400GBASE-R PHY with its own framing and cFEC — it is not an OTN/FlexO line-side mode. FlexO (ITU-T G.709.1/G.709.3) is a separate ITU-T beyond-100G OTN interface standard.

**Coherent optics ↔ IP routing**: With pluggable coherent optics in router ports, the traditional transponder/muxponder layer disappears. The router's interface directly produces a DWDM-compatible optical signal. This is the "IP-over-DWDM" or "routed optical networking" model.

## Edge Cases & Gotchas

**1. Modulation format vs reach — no free lunch**: Operators sometimes provision 16QAM on a path that can barely sustain it. The link works but has zero margin. Any fiber degradation (aging, new splices, temperature change) causes errors. Always design with margin.

**2. Laser linewidth matters for high-order QAM**: Phase noise from wide-linewidth lasers degrades 16QAM and above. QPSK tolerates ~1MHz linewidth; 64QAM needs <100kHz. External cavity lasers or ultra-narrow-linewidth designs are required for high-order modulation.

**3. Nonlinear interference at high launch power**: Coherent DSP compensates for linear impairments (CD, PMD) but cannot fully compensate for fiber nonlinearities. Pushing launch power to improve OSNR eventually hits the nonlinear threshold, beyond which performance degrades.

**4. Pluggable coherent power and thermal limits**: A 400ZR QSFP-DD module consumes 15-20W. A line card with 36 ports = 500-700W of optics alone. Thermal management (airflow, heat sinks) becomes a first-class design constraint.

**5. Multi-vendor interop**: 400ZR is standardized and interoperable. OpenZR+ is mostly interoperable but has vendor-specific extensions. Proprietary coherent modes (vendor-specific SD-FEC, framing) don't interoperate at all. Know which mode you're buying.
