# 9.1 — SP Transport Hierarchy — Answer Key

## Question 1
> **Your network has a long-haul route of 2,400 km. You need 400G per wavelength. What modulation format would you choose, and will you need regeneration? Explain your reasoning.**

### Answer

You'd use **DP-QPSK** at ~60 GBd, delivering ~200G per wavelength — not 400G. Here's why: 400G requires DP-16QAM, which has a typical transparent reach of 500–1,500 km. At 2,400 km, DP-16QAM doesn't close without regeneration. You'd need at minimum one — probably two — regen sites, which is a massive cost and complexity addition.

**The practical options:**

1. **DP-16QAM + 1–2 regen sites** — Gets you 400G per λ but adds O-E-O conversion sites at ~800 km and ~1,600 km. Each regen site needs a full transponder pair (receive, clean up, retransmit). Capex heavy.

2. **DP-QPSK at higher baud rate (~100+ GBd)** — Gives you 400G per λ with reach of 3,000+ km, clearing 2,400 km without regen. This requires a next-gen DSP (WaveLogic 6 class or equivalent) and wider spectral slots (~100–150 GHz), reducing total channel count per fiber. But zero regen sites = simpler operations.

3. **DP-8QAM with PCS** — A shaped 8QAM or shaped 16QAM constellation via probabilistic constellation shaping can hit ~300–350G at 2,400 km reach. PCS smoothly trades capacity for reach, so you might land at 350G without regen rather than 400G with regen.

**The right answer for most SPs:** Option 2 or 3. The cost of regen sites (real estate, power, sparing, management) almost always exceeds the cost of deploying fewer, wider channels. If you need exactly 400G, go high-baud DP-QPSK or DP-8QAM with PCS and accept fewer wavelengths per fiber. If you have fiber capacity to spare, the math is obvious.

**Will you need regeneration?** With DP-16QAM: yes. With DP-QPSK at high baud: no. With PCS-shaped 16QAM: depends on fiber quality, span losses, and amplifier chain — you're in the margin zone and need to run the link budget.

## Question 2
> **A metro ring has 6 ROADM nodes and is running at 90% wavelength capacity. A new customer needs 4 wavelengths between non-adjacent nodes. The optical team says they need to light a new fiber pair. The finance team asks why you can't "just add more channels." Explain the technical constraints.**

### Answer

The finance team is thinking about DWDM like a hotel — "just add more rooms." But at 90% capacity on a ring, you've hit several hard walls:

**1. Channel exhaustion on shared spans.** On a ring, every add/drop wavelength between non-adjacent nodes transits all intermediate spans. If you have 80 channels (standard C-band, 50 GHz grid) and 72 are in use (90%), you have 8 remaining. But those 8 free channels may not all be available on the *same* spans. Wavelength A might be free on span 1–2 but occupied on span 3–4. This is the **wavelength continuity constraint** — without wavelength conversion (which most metro ROADMs don't have), a wavelength must be the same color end-to-end.

**2. EDFA saturation.** Amplifiers have a maximum output power budget shared across all channels. At 90% fill, you're likely near the per-channel power limit. Adding 4 more wavelengths means reducing per-channel power slightly (rebalancing), which degrades OSNR on *all existing channels*. The optical team is rightfully nervous about tipping existing services below their FEC threshold.

**3. Nonlinear penalties.** More channels at high fill = more cross-phase modulation (XPM) and four-wave mixing (FWM) interference. These effects get worse nonlinearly as channel count increases. The last 10% of capacity costs disproportionately more in OSNR margin than the first 10%.

**4. ROADM pass-through penalties.** Each ROADM node adds ~0.5–1 dB of insertion loss per pass-through. On a 6-node ring, a non-adjacent connection might traverse 3–4 ROADMs. At 90% fill, the per-channel power penalty from ROADM cascading may have already consumed your margin.

**What the optical team is really saying:** "We can physically add 4 more wavelengths, but the margin analysis shows we'd be operating outside design limits on existing services, and we can't guarantee SLA-grade BER for the new or existing channels." Lighting a new fiber pair gives a clean system with full margin — which is the correct engineering decision.

## Question 3
> **Your IP links between two cities are showing intermittent CRC errors, but the optical team reports clean post-FEC BER. How do you investigate? What metrics would you ask the optical team to provide?**

### Answer

Post-FEC BER being clean just means FEC is doing its job correcting errors. It does NOT mean the optical path is healthy. The errors are happening — FEC is just papering over them. Your CRC errors at the IP layer are a different beast entirely.

**Investigation plan:**

**Step 1: Characterize the IP errors.** Are they constant or bursty? Time-correlated with anything? Check `show interface <x> | include CRC|errors|input` on both ends. CRC errors on a coherent link usually mean either (a) the FEC-corrected signal has micro-drops the router's Ethernet FCS catches, or (b) there's a client-side issue between router and transponder.

**Step 2: Ask the optical team for pre-FEC BER.** This is the critical metric. If pre-FEC BER is elevated (say 1e-4 to 5e-3) but stable, you have a degraded optical path that FEC is correcting — but you're eating into your FEC margin. If pre-FEC BER is spiking intermittently, that correlates with your CRC bursts.

**Step 3: Request pre-FEC BER trending** over 24–72 hours, sampled at 15-minute intervals minimum. Look for patterns — daily temperature cycles (outdoor amplifier huts), time-of-day correlation (vibration from nearby construction), or weather events.

**Step 4: Check the client-side optic** (grey optic between router and transponder). This is the most commonly overlooked failure point. The DWDM line side can be perfect while the short-reach grey optic between router and transponder has a dirty connector, marginal Tx power, or temperature drift. Run `show controllers optics <x>` and check Rx power, Tx power, laser bias current, and module temperature.

**Step 5: If pre-FEC is clean AND client optic is clean**, the problem is likely at Layer 2/3 — a software bug, a line card issue, or a configuration problem (MTU mismatch causing fragmentation that appears as CRC errors on the far end).

**Metrics to demand from the optical team:**
- Pre-FEC BER (current + 72hr history)
- OSNR at receiver
- CD and PMD readings from the coherent DSP
- Per-channel power levels at each ROADM
- Any recent maintenance or fiber events in the path

## Question 4
> **A junior engineer proposes running 1+1 optical protection on all metro ring wavelengths AND enabling TI-LFA on the IP layer. What's your recommendation and why?**

### Answer

Kill the 1+1 optical protection. Keep TI-LFA.

**Why:** 1+1 optical protection (OCh-SNCP/UPSR) reserves a dedicated protect path for every working wavelength. On a metro ring, that means 50% of your fiber capacity is sitting idle, waiting for a failure that statistically happens once a year. You're paying double for fiber, amplifiers, ROADM ports, and power — all for sub-50ms failover that TI-LFA already provides at the IP layer.

**TI-LFA gives you:**
- Sub-50ms convergence (pre-computed backup paths, loop-free)
- Zero reserved bandwidth — backup paths use existing capacity
- Granular per-prefix protection (not all-or-nothing per wavelength)
- Integration with your traffic engineering (SR-TE policies can define backup constraints)

**The trade-off the junior engineer is worried about:** TI-LFA operates at Layer 3, so it reacts to link-down events. If a fiber cut happens and the router's optic takes 100ms to declare link-down, TI-LFA starts 100ms late. 1+1 optical switches in ~10ms because it's monitoring optical power directly. Total recovery: optical = ~10ms, IP = ~100-150ms.

**Why that trade-off is acceptable:** 150ms recovery is imperceptible for virtually every application. VoIP handles 150ms fine. Video doesn't blink. TCP doesn't retransmit. The only applications that care about 10ms vs 150ms are high-frequency trading and real-time industrial control — and those customers should be paying for dedicated protection, not sharing a metro ring.

**The one exception:** If you have SLA commitments that explicitly require sub-20ms failover (some financial sector contracts), then you need optical protection on *those specific wavelengths* — not all of them. Selective 1+1 for premium services, TI-LFA for everything else.

**Bottom line:** Dual protection is paying for insurance twice. Pick the layer that gives you the best cost-efficiency, which in 2026 is almost always the IP layer.

## Question 5
> **Your SP is planning a new 800 km metro/regional network. Compare the trade-offs of using pluggable ZR+ optics in routers versus standalone transponders with a traditional DWDM line system.**

### Answer

800 km is the knife-edge distance where both approaches are technically viable but operationally very different.

**Pluggable ZR+ (OpenZR+, CFP2-DCO class):**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Reach | 400–800 km depending on vendor/fiber (marginal at 800 km — you're at the edge of the envelope) |
| Capacity | 400G per pluggable, potentially 100G/200G/300G with PCS |
| Capex | Lower — eliminates standalone transponder shelf (~$15-25K saved per endpoint pair) |
| Opex | Lower — one management domain (router NMS), fewer boxes, less rack space, less power |
| Density | Higher router port count = more wavelengths per RU |
| Flexibility | Locked to router platform upgrade cycles. New coherent generation = new router line card |
| Fault isolation | Harder — is the problem the router, the pluggable, or the line system? One box owns everything |
| Vendor lock-in | Less — OpenZR+ is a multi-source interop spec. Mix and match |
| Risk at 800 km | High — you're at the ragged edge of ZR+ reach. Fiber quality, span count, and connector cleanliness all become critical. Any degradation (aging, splice repair, new ROADM insertion) could push you below threshold |

**Standalone transponders + DWDM line system:**

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Reach | 800 km is comfortable. Plenty of margin with DP-8QAM or shaped 16QAM |
| Capacity | 400G–800G per wavelength with current-gen (WaveLogic 6, ICE7, PSE-6s) |
| Capex | Higher — separate transponder chassis, optics, and line system |
| Opex | Higher — two management domains, two vendor relationships, two sparing inventories |
| Density | Lower — separate shelf space for transponders |
| Flexibility | Higher — can upgrade transponder generation without touching routers. Can mix client rates (10G/100G/400G) on same line system |
| Fault isolation | Better — clear boundary between IP and optical. Separate PM counters, separate alarms |
| Vendor lock-in | More per-layer (Ciena line system = Ciena transponders, usually), but independent of router vendor |
| Risk at 800 km | Low — purpose-built for this distance. Margin to spare |

**My recommendation for 800 km:** Standalone transponders. At 800 km, you're beyond comfortable ZR+ territory, and the OSNR margin matters. If a fiber splice degrades by 2 dB in year 3, you don't want to be explaining to the CTO why your pluggable solution can't close the link anymore.

If this were a 300 km metro — pluggable ZR+ all day. The cost savings and operational simplicity are overwhelming at short distances where you have 10+ dB of margin to burn. But at 800 km, you're buying insurance with standalone transponders, and that insurance is cheap relative to the risk.
