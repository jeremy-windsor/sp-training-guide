# 9.2 — DWDM Fundamentals — Answer Key

---

## Question 1
> **Scenario**: You're deploying 400G DP-16QAM on a 12-span route (80 km spans, G.652 fiber). Each EDFA has 5.5 dB noise figure. The modulation format requires 18 dB OSNR. Calculate whether the link closes with adequate margin. What changes if you add Raman pre-amplification with 2 dB effective NF improvement?

### Answer

**Link budget calculation (EDFA-only):**

Using the OSNR formula: `OSNR ≈ P_launch - NF - 10·log₁₀(N) + 58`

First, determine the launch power. Standard per-channel launch power for a loaded DWDM system is typically +1 to +3 dBm. Let's use +1 dBm (conservative).

OSNR estimation requires summing accumulated ASE from each amplifier in the chain. Simplified vendor formulas exist but can overstate margin — always validate with vendor link-planning tools for production designs.

For a rough estimate: with 12 spans, +1 dBm launch, and 5.5 dB NF EDFAs, expect end-of-line OSNR in the low-to-mid 30s dB range (in 0.1 nm reference bandwidth) after accounting for real-world impairments.

Required OSNR for DP-16QAM: ~18 dB. Even with conservative estimates, a 12-span route has usable margin. **Real-world deductions to consider:**

| Penalty | Typical Value |
|---------|--------------|
| ROADM pass-through (assume 2 intermediate ROADMs) | 2–4 dB |
| Connector and splice losses (not captured in span gain=loss assumption) | 1–2 dB |
| Aging margin (component degradation over 10 years) | 2–3 dB |
| Nonlinear penalties (XPM/SPM at high channel loading) | 1–3 dB |
| Gain ripple / tilt accumulation across 12 EDFAs | 1–2 dB |
| System margin (design safety) | 3 dB |

Total real-world penalties: ~10–17 dB. Adjusted OSNR margin: 24.71 - 13 (midpoint) = **~11.7 dB**.

That's still healthy — the link closes with comfortable margin. 12 spans × 80 km = 960 km, which is within DP-16QAM's typical reach window (500–1,500 km).

**With Raman pre-amplification (2 dB NF improvement):**

Effective NF drops from 5.5 to 3.5 dB:

```
OSNR ≈ 1 - 3.5 - 10.79 + 58
     ≈ 44.71 dB
```

That's a 2 dB improvement in raw OSNR, which translates to roughly 25–40% additional reach or the ability to absorb more real-world penalties. The Raman doesn't just add 2 dB — it adds 2 dB per amplifier site, which is multiplicative across the chain. This is why Raman is a go-to tool for margin-challenged long-haul routes.

**Bottom line:** The link closes with EDFA-only. Raman buys you insurance margin and potentially allows higher launch power or additional wavelength loading without degrading existing channels.

## Question 2
> **Design**: Your network has a mix of 100G (DP-QPSK) and 400G (DP-16QAM) wavelengths sharing the same fiber. The legacy ROADMs use a fixed 50 GHz grid. What problems will you encounter with the 400G signals, and what's the upgrade path?

### Answer

You're about to learn why the optical industry invented flex-grid.

**The core problem:** A 400G DP-16QAM signal at ~64 GBaud occupies approximately **60–70 GHz** of spectral bandwidth (including roll-off and filtering margins). A 50 GHz grid slot gives you... 50 GHz. You're trying to park a pickup truck in a compact car space. The signal gets **clipped** by the 50 GHz filter passband of the ROADM's wavelength selective switch (WSS).

**Specific problems:**

1. **Filter narrowing / filter concatenation.** Each ROADM the 400G signal passes through applies a ~45 GHz effective passband (the 50 GHz slot minus guard bands). After cascading through 3–4 ROADMs, the effective passband narrows to ~35–40 GHz due to filter shape multiplication. The edges of your 400G signal get progressively shaved off, increasing ISI (inter-symbol interference) and degrading OSNR by 2–5 dB. Your 100G DP-QPSK signals (which occupy ~35 GHz) fit fine and cascade happily through many ROADMs.

2. **OSNR penalty.** The filter narrowing penalty means your 400G wavelengths need 2–5 dB more OSNR than they would on a flex-grid system. On marginal routes, this is the difference between the link closing and not closing.

3. **Reduced channel count.** If you force 400G onto a 50 GHz grid, you can do it with some DSP heroics (tight Nyquist shaping, reduced baud rate, aggressive PCS) — but you're leaving performance on the table. Alternatively, you can space 400G channels at 100 GHz (skip every other slot), which halves your usable channel count for 400G wavelengths.

**The upgrade path:**

| Option | Effort | Benefit |
|--------|--------|---------|
| **Replace WSS modules** to flex-grid capable (LCoS-based) | Medium — may require ROADM card swaps, not full chassis replacement | Enables 75 GHz and arbitrary slot widths. Best bang for the buck. |
| **Skip-slot allocation** — put 400G signals on every other 50 GHz slot (100 GHz effective) | Low — software/planning change only | Wastes half the spectrum for 400G but avoids hardware changes. Good interim solution. |
| **Full ROADM chassis replacement** (if legacy hardware doesn't support WSS swap) | High — new chassis, new amplifiers, full requalification | Future-proof for 800G+ and C+L band. Do this when the ROADMs are at end-of-life anyway. |
| **Deploy 400G only on point-to-point spans** (no ROADM cascade) | Low — design constraint only | Avoids filter narrowing entirely. Works if 400G is needed only on direct adjacencies. |

**The correct long-term answer:** Flex-grid ROADMs with 12.5 GHz granularity. Allocate 75 GHz slots for 400G (DP-16QAM at 64 GBaud) and 50 GHz for 100G (DP-QPSK). This gives you optimal spectral efficiency for mixed-rate systems and positions you for 800G (which needs ~150 GHz slots). The industry has standardized on this in ITU-T G.694.1 flex-grid amendment.

## Question 3
> **Troubleshooting**: A 100G DP-QPSK wavelength that's been stable for 2 years suddenly shows pre-FEC BER oscillating between 1e-4 and 5e-3 on a 12-hour cycle. OSNR is stable at 22 dB. What's your diagnosis and investigation plan?

### Answer

12-hour cycle with stable OSNR. That's a thermal signature. Something in the optical path is temperature-sensitive and cycling with day/night.

**Primary suspects:**

**1. Polarization Mode Dispersion (PMD) fluctuation.** PMD is inherently stochastic and temperature-dependent — the birefringence of fiber changes as temperature cycles. Older G.652 fiber (especially cable installed before ~2005) can have PMD values that marginally exceed the coherent DSP's compensation range during thermal extremes. The coherent receiver's adaptive equalizer compensates PMD dynamically, but if instantaneous DGD (differential group delay) spikes beyond ~30–40 ps at 100G, the DSP can't keep up and BER degrades.

**Why OSNR stays stable:** PMD doesn't affect optical power or ASE noise — it's a polarization effect. OSNR monitors measure total power, not polarization state. So you can have terrible PMD and perfect OSNR simultaneously.

**2. Polarization-Dependent Loss (PDL) in ROADM components.** WSS modules and isolators have some PDL (~0.2–0.5 dB each). As polarization state rotates with temperature, the PDL-induced penalty oscillates. Over a cascade of 4–5 ROADMs, accumulated PDL can reach 1–2 dB peak-to-peak, enough to cause the BER variation you're seeing.

**3. Fiber route exposed to thermal cycling.** Aerial fiber, fiber on bridges, or fiber in shallow conduits experiences wider temperature swings than buried fiber. A 30°C daily swing can cause measurable changes in chromatic dispersion, fiber strain, and polarization rotation.

**Investigation plan:**

1. **Pull DSP telemetry** — specifically CD, PMD/DGD, and PDL readings from the coherent receiver. Modern DSPs report these in real-time. If PMD correlates with BER oscillation, you've found it.

2. **Correlate with ambient temperature** at amplifier hut sites along the route. If BER peaks match afternoon high temperatures, you've confirmed thermal causation.

3. **Check for recent fiber route changes** — any spans rerouted through aerial cable, bridge crossings, or shallow conduit? These are PMD hotspots.

4. **OTDR the suspect spans** — look for splices or connectors that may have degraded. A bad splice that's thermally cycling could introduce periodic loss and reflection.

5. **If PMD is confirmed as root cause:** Options are (a) reduce baud rate / change to a more PMD-tolerant modulation if possible, (b) work with the fiber provider to identify and replace the high-PMD span sections, or (c) deploy a PMD compensator (rare, expensive, usually a last resort). Option (b) is the real fix.

**Key insight:** A link that worked fine for 2 years and suddenly develops a thermal cycle didn't change — something in the environment changed. New construction near the fiber route, a failed climate control system in an amplifier hut, or seasonal temperature extremes reaching a new threshold are all common triggers.
