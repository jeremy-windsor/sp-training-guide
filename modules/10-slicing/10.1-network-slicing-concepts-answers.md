# 10.1 — Network Slicing Concepts — Answer Key

---

## Question 1
> **Design**: A mobile operator asks you to support three slice types (eMBB, URLLC, MIoT) on your existing SR-MPLS backbone. They require <5ms one-way transport latency for URLLC and hard bandwidth isolation. Which realization model do you recommend, and why?

### Answer

You need a **hybrid realization model** — Flex-Algo for topology/latency isolation on URLLC, L3VPN with QoS for traffic separation across all three slices, and FlexE channelization at critical aggregation points for the "hard bandwidth isolation" requirement.

Here's the design:

**URLLC slice:** Flex-Algo 129 with metric-type `min-delay`. This computes shortest-latency paths across your SR-MPLS backbone, ignoring high-latency links that are fine for bulk traffic but death for URLLC. The <5ms one-way target is achievable on a well-designed metro/regional backbone — fiber propagation is ~5 μs/km, so 1,000 km of fiber = 5ms. Your real enemies are queuing delay and serialization, which Flex-Algo avoids by steering traffic onto low-hop, low-latency paths.

Wrap URLLC traffic in a dedicated L3VPN with strict-priority QoS (EF class). The VPN provides logical isolation; the QoS provides scheduling priority; Flex-Algo provides path optimization.

**eMBB slice:** Flex-Algo 128 (or default algo 0) with standard IGP metrics. eMBB wants bandwidth, not minimal latency. Route it on your normal ECMP paths with guaranteed bandwidth via weighted scheduling (WRR/DWRR). L3VPN for isolation.

**MIoT slice:** Default algorithm, best-effort QoS class with a guaranteed minimum bandwidth floor. MIoT traffic is low-bandwidth, latency-tolerant, and massive in scale (millions of endpoints, kilobit flows). Don't waste Flex-Algo resources on it — standard routing with basic QoS floors is sufficient. L3VPN for isolation.

**The "hard isolation" problem:** Flex-Algo + QoS gives you "enhanced-soft" isolation — it's very good but not absolute. Under extreme congestion or link failure, URLLC and eMBB still share physical links and buffers. If the contract demands *hard* bandwidth isolation (zero contention, guaranteed even during failures), you need FlexE channelization at the aggregation links where URLLC and eMBB traffic converges.

Deploy FlexE on the critical 2–4 links between aggregation and core routers. Channelize the 100G/400G links into dedicated slices: e.g., 20% for URLLC (guaranteed, never shared), 70% for eMBB, 10% for MIoT. This gives you TDM-style hard isolation where it matters most, without the cost and complexity of FlexE on every link in the backbone.

**What you're NOT doing:** Building three separate physical networks. That's the nuclear option — maximum isolation, maximum cost, maximum operational nightmare. The whole point of slicing is avoiding that.

## Question 2
> **Troubleshooting**: Your URLLC slice is meeting its latency SLO 99.7% of the time, but the customer requires 99.999%. Flex-Algo 129 (min-delay) is active on all nodes. What's your investigation approach?

### Answer

99.7% vs 99.999% means you're failing the SLO roughly 2,600 times per year instead of 5 times. Something is causing latency spikes that violate the target 0.3% of the time. Flex-Algo is doing its job 99.7% of the time — the remaining 0.3% is where the spikes live.

**Investigation approach, ordered by likelihood:**

**1. Identify WHEN the violations occur.**

Pull per-minute latency histograms for the URLLC path. Look for patterns:
- **Time-of-day correlation?** → Congestion from eMBB traffic spilling into URLLC queues during peak hours. Your QoS policy isn't strict enough — URLLC should be in a strict-priority queue, not weighted fair queuing.
- **Random spikes?** → Transient events: microloop during convergence, GC pause on a control plane process, or optical layer micro-cuts.
- **Periodic (every N minutes)?** → Control plane activity: IGP SPF recalculation, BGP scanner, telemetry polling, or OAM probe storms.

**2. Check for queuing delay at every hop.**

Even with Flex-Algo picking the shortest path, each node can introduce queuing delay. `show policy-map interface` (IOS-XR) or `show class-of-service interface` (Junos) at every hop in the Flex-Algo path. Look for:
- URLLC traffic NOT in strict-priority queue → fix the QoS policy
- Queue drops or tail-drop events on the URLLC queue → the queue is too small or traffic exceeds the committed rate
- High-priority control plane traffic (BFD, IGP hellos) sharing the strict-priority queue → isolate them

**3. Check for Flex-Algo path changes.**

If a link in the Flex-Algo 129 topology goes down, traffic reroutes to the next-best min-delay path. During convergence (even with TI-LFA), there's a brief period where traffic may take a suboptimal path or experience a microloop. If this happens during the 0.3% failure window:
- Check IGP convergence events in the Flex-Algo topology
- Check TI-LFA backup path latency — is the backup path also within SLO? If your backup path goes through a high-latency link, every failover event violates the SLO.
- Solution: Constrain TI-LFA backup computation to only use links participating in Flex-Algo 129. This ensures even failure paths meet the min-delay constraint.

**4. Check for ECMP asymmetry.**

If Flex-Algo 129 has multiple equal-cost min-delay paths, traffic is ECMP hashed. If one of those ECMP paths traverses a node with more congestion or more hops, some flows get higher latency than others. 99.7% of flows hash to the fast path; 0.3% hash to the slow one.

Solution: Verify all ECMP paths in the Flex-Algo topology have equivalent latency. If not, adjust IGP delay metrics or use SR-TE policies with explicit path constraints instead of ECMP.

**5. Check serialization delay on low-speed links.**

If any link in the Flex-Algo path is 1G or 10G (common in access aggregation), serialization delay for large packets (9000-byte jumbo frames) adds 72 μs on 1G or 7.2 μs on 10G. Multiple low-speed hops compound this. URLLC traffic should be on 100G+ links wherever possible.

**6. Verify the delay metrics are accurate.**

Flex-Algo 129 min-delay relies on IGP TE delay metrics (RFC 8570, which obsoleted RFC 7810). If these metrics are stale, manually configured (not measured), or based on one-time measurements, the "shortest delay" path might not actually be the shortest delay path. Deploy dynamic delay measurement (PM delay measurement via TWAMP or hardware timestamping) and feed live values into IGP.

**The 99.999% answer:** You probably need a combination of fixes — strict-priority QoS, Flex-Algo backup path constraints, dynamic delay measurement, and possibly dedicated FlexE channels on the critical hops. Five-nines latency SLO is brutal, and achieving it requires eliminating every source of variance, not just optimizing the average.

## Question 3
> **Architecture**: The marketing team wants to sell 50 unique "network slices" to enterprise customers. The engineering team pushes back. Explain why 50 Flex-Algos is a terrible idea and propose an alternative architecture that scales.

### Answer

Marketing needs to meet engineering in the middle, because 50 Flex-Algos would set your network on fire.

**Why 50 Flex-Algos is a terrible idea:**

1. **Control plane explosion.** Each Flex-Algo runs a separate SPF computation on every router in the domain. 50 Flex-Algos = 50 independent topology databases, 50 SPF runs per convergence event, 50 sets of backup path computations. On a 200-node backbone, that's 10,000 SPF calculations per topology change. IS-IS/OSPF wasn't designed for this — you'll see CPU spikes, convergence delays, and potentially protocol instability.

2. **SRGB/SRLB exhaustion.** Each Flex-Algo allocates a prefix SID per node. With 200 nodes × 50 Flex-Algos, you need 10,000 SIDs just for node SIDs. The default SRGB range (16000–23999) gives you 8,000. You're already short. Even with expanded SRGB, you're consuming label space that could be used for service SIDs.

3. **Operational nightmare.** 50 separate topologies to monitor, troubleshoot, and capacity-plan. When a link goes down, you need to understand the impact on 50 different forwarding planes. Your NOC will revolt.

4. **Diminishing returns.** Do 50 enterprise customers actually have 50 *different* transport requirements? No. They have maybe 4–6 distinct profiles: low-latency, high-bandwidth, diverse-path, best-effort, guaranteed-bandwidth, and maybe one or two exotic ones. Marketing is selling "unique" slices, but engineering should deliver a small number of *transport profiles* that meet the actual requirements.

**The architecture that scales:**

**Layer 1 — Transport profiles (3–6 Flex-Algos):**

| Flex-Algo | Metric | Use Case |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| 0 (default) | IGP cost | Best-effort, standard routing |
| 128 | TE metric (bandwidth-aware) | High-bandwidth services |
| 129 | Min-delay | Low-latency services |
| 130 | IGP cost + exclude-SRLG constraint | Diverse-path services |
| 131 | Min-delay + include-only (premium nodes) | Ultra-premium low-latency |

5 Flex-Algos, not 50. Each one represents a genuinely different transport behavior.

**Layer 2 — Per-customer L3VPN with QoS:**

Each enterprise customer gets a dedicated L3VPN (unique RD/RT). The VPN is mapped to the appropriate Flex-Algo via SR-TE color/BGP-CT. QoS policies provide per-customer bandwidth guarantees within the shared Flex-Algo topology.

This is where marketing gets their "50 unique slices" — each customer has their own VPN with their own SLA parameters (bandwidth commitment, burst rate, latency target). The VPN is steered onto the appropriate Flex-Algo profile. From the customer's perspective, it IS a unique slice. From engineering's perspective, it's one of 5 transport profiles with per-VPN QoS.

**Layer 3 — SR-TE policies for special cases:**

If a customer needs something truly unique (explicit path through specific nodes, latency < X AND bandwidth > Y simultaneously), use an SR-TE on-demand policy with multi-constraint path computation. This handles the 2% of customers who don't fit the standard profiles, without burning a Flex-Algo for each one.

**Tell marketing:** "We sell 50 slices. We build 5 transport profiles. The mapping is our problem, not the customer's."

## Question 4
> **Standards**: What is the difference between an "IETF Network Slice" (RFC 9543) and a "3GPP Network Slice" (TS 23.501)? Why does this distinction matter for transport engineers?

### Answer

They sound like the same thing. They are not. And confusing them will cause you real problems in multi-vendor, multi-domain deployments.

**3GPP Network Slice (TS 23.501):**

A 3GPP slice is an **end-to-end logical network** spanning RAN, transport, and core. It's identified by S-NSSAI (SST + SD) and includes:
- RAN resources (radio scheduling, beamforming, PRB allocation)
- Core network functions (dedicated or shared UPF, AMF, SMF instances)
- Transport network resources (your domain)
- Management and orchestration (NSMF, NSSMF per 3GPP TS 28.530)

A 3GPP slice is a **service concept** — it defines *what* the customer gets (eMBB, URLLC, MIoT) across the entire mobile network. The transport portion is just one segment of the end-to-end slice.

**IETF Network Slice (RFC 9543):**

An IETF network slice is a **transport-domain construct** — a set of connectivity constructs between endpoints with defined SLOs (latency, bandwidth, availability, isolation). It's technology-agnostic: could be realized with SR-TE, MPLS, RSVP-TE, FlexE, or even static routes. It's defined by:
- Service Demarcation Points (where traffic enters/exits the slice)
- SLOs (measurable performance guarantees)
- SLEs (desired but best-effort attributes)
- Connectivity constructs (the logical topology within the slice)

An IETF slice is a **resource abstraction** — it defines *how* the transport network allocates and isolates resources to meet the SLOs.

**The key differences:**

| Dimension | 3GPP Slice | IETF Slice |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Scope | End-to-end (RAN + Transport + Core) | Transport domain only |
| Identity | S-NSSAI (SST + SD) | Slice identifier (local to NSC) |
| Lifecycle | Managed by NSMF/NSSMF | Managed by Network Slice Controller |
| Technology | Implies 5G NR, SBA core | Technology-agnostic |
| Customer | Mobile subscriber (via UE slice selection) | 3GPP management system, enterprise, or another NSC |
| Granularity | Per-service-type (eMBB/URLLC/MIoT) | Per-connectivity-construct (arbitrary SLOs) |

**Why this matters for transport engineers:**

1. **You implement IETF slices, not 3GPP slices.** The 3GPP management system sends you a request that says "I need a transport path between site A and site B with <5ms latency, 10 Gbps guaranteed, 99.999% availability." That's an IETF Network Slice request. You don't care that it's for URLLC or eMBB — you care about the SLOs.

2. **Multiple 3GPP slices can share one IETF slice.** If three enterprise customers each have a URLLC 3GPP slice with identical transport SLOs, you don't build three separate transport slices. You build one IETF slice (one Flex-Algo topology, one QoS profile) and map all three 3GPP slices onto it. This is exactly the "50 slices → 5 profiles" pattern from Question 3.

3. **One 3GPP slice may need multiple IETF slices.** An eMBB slice that spans two transport domains (metro + long-haul) may require two IETF slices stitched together at the domain boundary, each with different SLOs and potentially different realization technologies.

4. **Interoperability lives at the IETF layer.** When your transport domain peers with another operator's transport domain (inter-AS, multi-domain), the slice handoff uses IETF abstractions and protocols (ACTN framework, RFC 8453), not 3GPP signaling. 3GPP slice IDs don't cross transport domain boundaries — SLOs do.

**Bottom line:** 3GPP tells you what the customer wants. IETF tells you how to build it. If you're a transport engineer, your API is RFC 9543, not TS 23.501.
