# 8.1 — Legacy L2VPN: VPWS, VPLS, and H-VPLS — Answer Key

---

## Question 1
> **Design:** A customer needs multipoint L2 connectivity across 8 sites, with all-active dual-homing at 2 sites. Why can't VPLS deliver this, and what specific EVPN mechanism solves it?

### Answer

**Short answer:** classic VPLS can provide the **8-site multipoint LAN**, but it cannot provide **standards-based all-active multi-homing** at the two dual-homed sites. That is exactly where EVPN replaces it.

### Why VPLS falls short

VPLS is fundamentally a **data-plane MAC learning** technology with split-horizon between pseudowires. It can emulate a multipoint bridge across 8 sites, but for dual-homing it runs into three problems:

1. **No proper all-active multi-homing control plane**  
   VPLS does not have a native equivalent of EVPN's Ethernet Segment model. The usual outcome is active/standby with STP, MC-LAG tricks, or vendor-specific designs.

2. **No aliasing / fast mass withdrawal**  
   If one PE in a dual-homed pair fails, VPLS does not have a compact control-plane signal that says, “remove this PE as a next-hop for every MAC on this segment.” Recovery is slower and more operationally ugly.

3. **Split-horizon is too blunt**  
   VPLS split-horizon prevents PW-to-PW loops, but it does not solve EVPN's per-Ethernet-Segment forwarding problem where multiple PEs intentionally attach to the same CE and must coordinate BUM and unicast behavior.

### What EVPN provides instead

EVPN solves this with the **Ethernet Segment Identifier (ESI)** plus the **EVPN multi-homing route types**:

- **Route Type 4 (Ethernet Segment route):** used for **DF election** so only one PE forwards BUM traffic toward the CE for a given VLAN / Ethernet Tag.
- **Route Type 1 (EAD per-ES / per-EVI):** used for **mass withdrawal** and **aliasing**.
- **ESI label / split-horizon logic:** prevents loops for all-active multi-homed segments.

### The specific mechanism to name on an exam

If the question asks for the **specific EVPN mechanism**, the best full-credit answer is:

> **EVPN all-active multi-homing using a shared ESI, Route Type 4 DF election for BUM, and Route Type 1 aliasing / mass withdrawal for fast failover.**

That is the clean answer because it names both the architectural object (**ESI**) and the operational machinery (**RT-4 DF election, RT-1 aliasing/mass withdrawal**).

### Operational outcome

With EVPN:
- both PE uplinks at the dual-homed sites can forward **unicast** in all-active mode,
- only the DF forwards **BUM** toward the CE,
- remote PEs can ECMP toward either PE due to **aliasing**,
- failure of one PE triggers **mass withdrawal** rather than per-MAC churn.

That is why EVPN is not just “better VPLS”; it is the first L2VPN control plane that actually handles multi-homing like a grown-up.

## Question 2
> **Troubleshooting:** You configure a VPWS between two PEs. The PW shows "UP" on both sides, but traffic only flows in one direction. Both ACs are up. What's the most likely cause, and how do you confirm?

### Answer

The most likely cause is an **attachment-circuit encapsulation mismatch** — usually a **VLAN tagging mismatch** (one side is raw Ethernet / `ethernet-ccc`, the other side expects tagged `vlan-ccc`, or the VLAN IDs do not match).

Why this is the best first answer:
- the **pseudowire signaling can still come up** because the PE loopbacks, VC ID/PW ID, and signaling are correct,
- both **ACs can show link-up** physically,
- but the actual customer frames are not being interpreted the same way on both ends, so data passes only one direction or appears black-holed.

MTU mismatch is another common culprit, but it more often presents as **selective traffic failure** (small frames work, larger frames do not) rather than a clean one-way symptom.

### What to confirm

Check the negotiated / configured PW parameters and the AC encapsulation on **both** PEs.

#### IOS-XR
```cisco
show l2vpn xconnect detail
show l2vpn xconnect group <group> p2p <name> detail
show interface <ce-facing-interface>
show running-config l2vpn
```

Look for:
- PW ID / VC ID match
- encapsulation type
- control word state
- MTU
- CE-facing interface encapsulation (`l2transport`, VLAN subif, rewrite rules)

#### Junos
```junos
show l2circuit connections extensive
show configuration protocols l2circuit
show configuration interfaces <ce-facing-interface>
show interfaces terse | match up
```

Look for:
- `encapsulation ethernet-ccc` vs `vlan-ccc`
- correct `vlan-id`
- matching neighbor + VC ID
- interface family / encapsulation matching the service design

### Fast diagnostic logic

1. **If small and large frames both fail one way** → suspect VLAN / AC mismatch first.  
2. **If small frames work but larger ones fail** → suspect MTU mismatch.  
3. **If traffic is intermittent under ECMP** → suspect control-word mismatch / hashing behavior.

### Best exam answer

A strong exam answer says:

> The PW is up because control-plane signaling is correct, but the **AC encapsulation is mismatched** (usually tagged vs untagged, or wrong VLAN). Confirm with `show l2vpn xconnect detail` / `show l2circuit connections extensive` and compare CE-facing interface encapsulation, VLAN handling, control-word, and MTU on both PEs.

That shows you understand the difference between **PW state** and **actual end-to-end service correctness**.

## Question 3
> **Scale:** You're operating H-VPLS with 5 core PEs and 40 MTUs. An engineer proposes eliminating H-VPLS and using flat VPLS with BGP auto-discovery instead. Calculate the PW count for both designs and explain the trade-offs beyond raw PW count.

### Answer

### PW count calculation

You currently have **H-VPLS** with:
- **5 core PEs** running the full VPLS mesh
- **40 MTUs** connected as spoke nodes

#### H-VPLS

Core full mesh:
- `5 × (5 - 1) / 2 = 10` core pseudowires

Spoke PWs:
- `40` MTUs × `1` spoke PW each = `40` pseudowires

**Total H-VPLS PW count = 10 + 40 = 50 PWs**

#### Flat VPLS with BGP auto-discovery

If you flatten the design, every node becomes a VPLS PE participating in the full mesh:
- total nodes = `5 + 40 = 45`
- full mesh PW count = `45 × 44 / 2 = 990` pseudowires

**Total flat VPLS PW count = 990 PWs**

So purely on PW count, the proposal is catastrophic:
- **H-VPLS = 50 PWs**
- **Flat VPLS = 990 PWs**

### But the trade-offs are worse than just the number

#### 1. BGP auto-discovery does not fix the data-plane scale problem

BGP auto-discovery (RFC 4761) improves **discovery/signaling**, not the fact that flat VPLS still behaves like a giant distributed Ethernet switch with:
- full-mesh PW state,
- data-plane MAC learning,
- ingress replication of BUM to every remote PE,
- no real control-plane MAC intelligence.

So yes, you avoid some targeted-LDP session management pain, but you keep the architectural problem.

#### 2. BUM blast radius becomes much larger

With 45 PEs in one flat VPLS, every broadcast / unknown unicast / multicast frame is replicated broadly across the mesh. A bad CE loop or storm now has a much larger failure domain.

This is exactly the kind of design that turns one stupid access switch into a backbone-wide emotional event.

#### 3. Access nodes get heavier

In H-VPLS, MTUs are deliberately simple:
- they terminate a spoke PW,
- they do not participate in the full VPLS mesh.

In flat VPLS, those 40 MTUs now become full participants in:
- PW mesh state,
- MAC learning state,
- BUM replication state,
- larger control-plane and operational complexity.

That is the opposite of good hierarchy.

#### 4. Troubleshooting and maintenance get uglier

H-VPLS gives you architectural separation:
- **core PEs** handle the service mesh,
- **MTUs** stay closer to access simplification.

Flattening removes that hierarchy and increases:
- fault domain size,
- convergence churn,
- change risk,
- operational coupling.

### Bottom line

A full-credit answer is:

> H-VPLS uses `5×4/2 = 10` core PWs plus `40` spoke PWs, for **50 total**. Flat VPLS across 45 nodes needs `45×44/2 = 990` PWs. BGP auto-discovery reduces discovery complexity but does **not** solve the fundamental scaling problems of flat VPLS: larger BUM replication domain, larger MAC tables, heavier access nodes, and a much wider failure blast radius. H-VPLS exists precisely to avoid this collapse.

And then the adult conclusion:

> If scale pain is high enough that you're questioning H-VPLS, the real destination is usually **EVPN**, not “bigger VPLS.”
